# Consideration

As a rule, a promise has no binding force unless supported by **consideration** or made by deed.
- Definition: something of value in the eyes of the law, given in return for the promise → *Gay Choon Ing v Loh*
- Test of value: benefit-detriment analysis (benefit to the promisor or detriment to the promisee) → *Currie v Misa [1875]*

## The 3 requirements

### 1. Requested by the promisor
A voluntary detriment is not consideration → *Combe v Combe [1951]*

### 2. Must move from the promisee
Only a promisee who provides consideration can enforce → *Tweddle v Atkinson [1861]*
- **Joint Promisees**: one of several joint promisees may provide consideration on behalf of the others → *Coulls v Bagot's Executor [1960]*

### 3. Past consideration is no consideration
- **Pao On exception** (adopted in Singapore: *Sim Tony v Lim Ah Ghee [1995]*): (1) the act was done at the promisor's request, (2) it was understood that there would be compensation, (3) it would have been enforceable had it been promised in advance → a past act can be consideration

### Sufficient not adequate
Consideration need only be sufficient; it need not be adequate → *Chappell v Nestle [1960]*

## Special Situations

| Situation | Principle | Authority |
|------|------|------|
| Intangible / moral obligation | Love and affection are insufficient; a moral obligation is also insufficient | *Thomas v Thomas [1842]*, *White v Bluett [1853]* |
| Public / legal duty | Mere performance is insufficient; exceeding it is sufficient | *Glasbrook Bros v Glamorgan CC [1925]*, *Ward v Byham [1955]* |
| Existing duty owed to a third party | Is good consideration | *Scotson v Pegg [1861]* |
| **Existing duty to the promisor — to receive more** | Principle: insufficient / Exception: sufficient where a practical benefit is secured (provided there is no duress or fraud) | *Stilk v Myrick [1809]* / *Williams v Roffey Bros [1991]* (SG: *Teo Seng Kee Bob v Arianecorp [2008]*) |
| **Existing duty to the promisor — to receive less** | Principle: part payment is not consideration for discharge of the whole debt / Exception: where something different is provided | *Pinnel's Case [1602]*, *Foakes v Beer [1884]* |

## Promissory Estoppel

> An equitable principle that prevents a party from going back on a promise even where it is not supported by sufficient consideration → *Central London Property Trust v High Trees House [1946]*

**4 elements** (*Lam Chi Kin David v Deutsche Bank AG [2011]*):
1. Pre-existing legal relations
2. A clear and unconditional promise (representation) not to enforce one's legal rights
3. The promisee's reliance on the promise
4. It must be inequitable for the promisor to go back on the promise

**Key point**: In Singapore it may be used **only as a shield, not as a sword** (in Australia it may be used as a sword — *Waltons v Maher [1998]*)

**Nature**:
- As a rule, **suspensory** — the obligation may revive after reasonable notice → *Hughes v Metropolitan Railway [1877]*
- Exception: where it is impossible/inequitable, the past obligation is extinguished → *High Trees [1947]*
- Periodic payments: amounts accrued before notice are extinguished, future amounts are suspended
- One-off payments: depends on the creditor's intention (full release vs. extension of time)
