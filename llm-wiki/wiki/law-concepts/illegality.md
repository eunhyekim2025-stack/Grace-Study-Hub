# Illegality

A contract that is illegal or contrary to public policy is unenforceable.

## Classification

### 1. Statutory Illegality
- **Express prohibition**: the statute renders the contract itself void
- **Implied prohibition**: void in light of the statute's purpose → *Re Mahmoud and Ispahani [1921]*

### 2. Common Law Illegality (contrary to public policy)
| Type | Example |
|------|------|
| Purpose of crime / tort | Contract to commit fraud or a crime |
| Interference with justice | Consideration for withdrawing a suit, etc. |
| Public corruption | Sale of public office |
| Sexual immorality | *Pearce v Brooks [1866]* |
| Interference with marriage / family | Contract in restraint of marriage |
| **Restraint of Trade** | [[wiki/law-concepts/restraint-of-trade]] |

### 3. Intermediate Cases — Singapore
The contract itself is lawful but is performed with an illegal intent/method. The court weighs **proportionality** → *Ting Siew May v Boon Lay Choo [2014] SGCA*

---

## Consequences

- Principle: an illegal contract is unenforceable (*ex turpi causa non oritur actio*)
- Damages and restitution are generally unavailable (*in pari delicto potior est conditio defendentis*)
- **Exceptions**: (1) the parties are not equally at fault, (2) repentance before the illegal purpose is carried out, (3) a claim that can be made without relying on the illegality
- Singapore's restitution approach: *Ochroid Trading v Chua Siok Lui [2018] SGCA*
