# Misrepresentation

A **false statement of fact** that induces the formation of a contract. A vitiating factor that renders a contract voidable.

## The 4 elements

1. **False statement of fact**
   - Opinions are generally excluded → *Bisset v Wilkinson [1927]*
   - But an expert opinion / an opinion with no basis is a statement of fact → *Smith v Land & House Property [1884]*
   - Future intention: if there was no such intention at the time, it is a misrepresentation of fact → *Edgington v Fitzmaurice [1885]*
   - **Silence** is generally not a representation, except: half-truths / change of circumstances / fiduciary relationships → *With v O'Flanagan [1936]*
2. **Communicated to the representee**
3. **The other party relied on it (reliance)** → *Attwood v Small [1838]* (reliance denied where the party conducted its own investigation)
4. **The other party was induced** → sufficient if it was a material factor → *Edgington v Fitzmaurice*

---

## Types

| Type | State of mind | Basis for damages | Authority |
|------|-----------|---------------|------|
| **Fraudulent** | Knows it is false or is recklessly indifferent | Tort of deceit | *Derry v Peek [1889]* |
| **Negligent** | Fails to prove a reasonable basis for believing it true | **Misrepresentation Act s.2(1)** | *Howard Marine v Ogden [1978]* |
| **Innocent** | Believed it true on reasonable grounds | Discretionary damages under s.2(2) in lieu of rescission | — |

> Misrepresentation Act s.2(1) shifts the burden of proof onto the representor — very favourable to the victim.

---

## Remedies

- **Rescission**: voids the contract from the outset, with restitution
- **Bars to rescission**:
  - Affirmation, lapse of time, restitution impossible, third party rights acquired
- **Damages**: by type (see table above)
