# Capacity

Certain parties have limited contractual capacity, which may render a contract void or voidable.

## 1. Minors (under 18)

Principle: a minor's contract is **not binding on the minor** (the other party is bound). Exceptions:

| Type | Effect | Authority |
|------|------|------|
| **Necessaries** | Binding — obligation to pay a reasonable price (SOGA s.3) | *Nash v Inman [1908]* |
| **Beneficial contracts of service (employment / education)** | Binding if beneficial on the whole | *Doyle v White City Stadium [1935]* |
| **Voidable contracts (continuing obligations: land, shares, partnership)** | Binding if not repudiated before or shortly after reaching majority | *Steinberg v Scala [1923]* |
| **Other (e.g. purchase of goods)** | Not binding until ratified | — |

- **Minors' Contracts Act**: restitution available in equity

## 2. Mental Incapacity

- Where a person cannot understand the meaning of the contract + the other party knew of this → voidable → *Imperial Loan v Stone [1892]*
- The Mental Capacity Act applies
- Necessaries still attract an obligation to pay a reasonable price

## 3. Intoxication
Treated similarly to mental incapacity — voidable where the other party knew and the person could not understand.

## 4. Corporations
- A company has capacity within the scope of its constitution
- **Ultra vires (acts beyond capacity)**: formerly void, but under **Companies Act s.25** third parties are now protected — the validity of an act cannot be challenged merely because it is ultra vires
