# Undue Influence

An equitable vitiating factor. One party abuses a position of dominance in a relationship to obtain consent → the contract is **voidable**.

## Classification (*BCCI v Aboody [1990]* / *Royal Bank of Scotland v Etridge [2001]*)

### Class 1 — Actual Undue Influence
The actual exercise of improper pressure is **proven**. → *Williams v Bayley [1866]*

### Class 2 — Presumed Undue Influence
A relationship of trust and confidence + a suspicious transaction → influence is presumed, and the other party must rebut it.

| Sub-class | Content | Examples |
|-----------|------|------|
| **Class 2A** | Relationships the law automatically recognises as confidential | parent-child, doctor-patient, solicitor-client, trustee-beneficiary (but **not** husband and wife) |
| **Class 2B** | A relationship of trust and dependence proven on the facts | *Lloyds Bank v Bundy [1975]* |

**Additional requirement**: the transaction must be one that "calls for explanation" → *Etridge*

---

## Doctrine of Notice / Bank Cases (Third Party)

Where a surety alleges undue influence by the debtor, the transaction may be set aside if the bank knew or ought to have known of it.
- The bank's protective steps: advise the surety to obtain **independent legal advice** → *Etridge* guidelines
- Singapore application: *Bank of East Asia v Mody Sonal [2004]*

---

## Effects
- The contract is voidable; rescission available
- Bars: affirmation, lapse of time, restitution impossible, third party rights
