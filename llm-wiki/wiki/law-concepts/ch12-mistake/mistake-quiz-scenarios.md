# Chapter 8 · Mistake — Scenario Quiz (PQ Style)

> Same approach as the PQ: read the scenario and answer in the order ① **identify the Issue** → ② **Rule** → ③ **Application** → ④ **Conclusion**.
> Check yourself with the **Model Answer** below each card.

---

## PART A · Issue Spotting (What Kind of Mistake?)

---

### Scenario A1 — Two Ships

Tony and Stark contracted to buy and sell "the steel loaded on the Peerless" for $50,000. Tony had in mind the Peerless sailing in March, while Stark had in mind the Peerless sailing in November. The contract simply states "the Peerless."

**Q: What type of Mistake is this? What is the effect on the contract?**

<details>
<summary>▶ Model Answer</summary>

**Issue**: Whether there is Mutual Mistake

**Rule**: Mutual Mistake occurs where the two parties each have something different in mind as to offer and acceptance, so there is no meeting of the minds. Where they disagree on a fundamental aspect, the contract is void (*Raffles v Wichelhaus [1864]*).

**Application**:
- Tony = March Peerless / Stark = November Peerless → entirely different subject matter
- "Which Peerless" is a fundamental aspect of this contract
- The contract contains no identifying indication → not interpretable objectively either

**Conclusion**: Mutual Mistake → **contract void**. Equity cannot intervene (since there is no contract at all).

</details>

---

### Scenario A2 — The Ship Already Gone

Janet contracted to sell mask stock held in a warehouse to Wanda for $10,000. At the time of contracting, neither party knew, but the mask stock had already been entirely destroyed in a warehouse fire.

**Q: What type of Mistake is this? What is the conclusion?**

<details>
<summary>▶ Model Answer</summary>

**Issue**: Common Mistake — whether Res Extincta applies

**Rule**: Res Extincta — where the subject matter has already perished at the time of contracting and both parties are unaware, the contract is void (*Couturier v Hastie [1856]*). An exception applies where one party warrants the risk (*McRae v Commonwealth [1951]*).

**Application**:
- At the time of contracting, the masks no longer existed
- Both Janet and Wanda were unaware of the destruction → Common Mistake (sharing the same false assumption)
- No risk-allocation clause in the contract, and Janet did not warrant existence

**Conclusion**: Common Mistake (Res Extincta) → **contract void ab initio**. Janet cannot claim the price, and Wanda is under no obligation to perform.

</details>

---

### Scenario A3 — Something I Already Owned

Stan contracted to sell an apartment he owned to Morgan. As it turned out, Morgan had already been the legal owner of that apartment for 10 years through an inheritance from his parents, and neither party knew this.

**Q: What type of Mistake is this? What is the conclusion?**

<details>
<summary>▶ Model Answer</summary>

**Issue**: Common Mistake — whether Res Sua applies

**Rule**: Res Sua — a contract where the buyer purchases something he already owns. Where both parties are unaware of the ownership position → the contract is void.

**Application**:
- Morgan is already the legal owner of the apartment
- Both Stan and Morgan were unaware of this → common mistake
- The situation of "selling/buying one's own thing" → Res Sua applies

**Conclusion**: Common Mistake (Res Sua) → **contract void**. Since Morgan is already the owner, there can be no transfer.

</details>

---

### Scenario A4 — Mistake as to Quality; Is the Contract Alive?

Tony contracted to buy "top-grade carbon-steel alloy" worth $2M from the Nibelung steelworks. After concluding the contract, both parties belatedly discovered that the material delivered was in fact ordinary steel (not the top-grade alloy). The top-grade alloy and ordinary steel differ in strength, but both are usable in Tony's manufacturing process.

**Q: Is Common Mistake made out? Is the contract void?**

<details>
<summary>▶ Model Answer</summary>

**Issue**: Whether Common Mistake (Broader Doctrine — Quality) is made out

**Rule** (*Bell v Lever Brothers [1932]*): A mistake as to quality is applied **extremely narrowly** at Common law. It is void only if the mistake makes the subject matter "essentially and radically different." A mere difference in quality or value is not enough.

**Application**:
- Ordinary steel ≠ top-grade alloy → a difference in quality exists
- But **both are usable** in Tony's process → the very purpose of the contract has not become impossible
- Hard to regard the subject matter as "radically different" → the Bell v Lever test is not satisfied

**Conclusion**: Common Mistake **not made out**. Contract **valid**. Tony bears the obligation to perform, and as for the quality difference he must consider other remedies such as Terms/Misrepresentation.

> **Exam tip**: The Bell test is deliberately narrow. If "the purpose can still be achieved," Common Mistake generally fails.

</details>

## PART B · Unilateral Mistake — Term of Contract

---

### Scenario B1 — Price Typo (CL vs Equity)

Borson runs an online shopping mall. Due to a staff error, a $3,000 laptop was displayed as $30. Hela, knowing this price was an obvious error, immediately ordered 100 units. Siegmund simply thought it was cheap and ordered 1 unit.

**Q: (a) What is the effect on Hela's contract? (b) What is the effect on Siegmund's contract?**

<details>
<summary>▶ Model Answer</summary>

**Issue**: Unilateral Mistake as to a term (price)

**Rule** (*Chwee Kin Keong v Digilandmall [2005]*, *Smith v Hughes [1871]*):
- Mistake as to a material term + **actual knowledge** → CL → **void**
- Mistake as to a material term + **constructive knowledge + unconscionable conduct** → Equity → **voidable**

**(a) Hela's case**:
- $30 vs $3,000 — a 100× difference → an obvious pricing error
- Hela ordered 100 units "knowing" → **actual knowledge** found
- → CL Unilateral Mistake → **contract void**

**(b) Siegmund's case**:
- A reasonable person should have known a $30 laptop was an error → **constructive knowledge**
- But he merely thought it was cheap → active unconscionable conduct (impropriety) is hard to establish
- The equitable remedy is uncertain. The court may infer impropriety from the extremity of the price difference alone (cf. *Chwee*)
- Conclusion: possibly **voidable** in Equity, but the remedy is less likely to be made out than for Hela

> **Key distinction**: actual knowledge (CL, void) vs constructive knowledge + unconscionability (Equity, voidable)

</details>

### Scenario B2 — The Caveat Emptor Trap

Donald sells used cars. Thor came and asked, "Is this car's engine good?" Donald knew the engine was in bad condition but said nothing, and Thor thought to himself "it must be fine" and concluded the contract. Thor later incurred $5,000 in repair costs because the engine was in bad condition.

**Q: Can Thor plead Unilateral Mistake?**

<details>
<summary>▶ Model Answer</summary>

**Issue**: Unilateral Mistake (term) — whether Donald's silence affects whether a mistake is made out

**Rule**:
- Unilateral Mistake: ① a mistake as to a material term ② the non-mistaken party knew or should have known of the mistake
- **Caveat Emptor principle**: the seller is under **no duty** to correct the buyer's self-induced misjudgement (*Smith v Hughes [1871]*)
- Mere silence ≠ "inducing" the mistake (cf. Misrepresentation is different)

**Application**:
- Thor's mistake = his own assumption about the engine's condition → self-induced misjudgement
- Donald merely stayed silent → no active conduct inducing the mistake
- Caveat Emptor applies → Donald has no duty to correct

**Conclusion**: Unilateral Mistake **not made out**. Contract valid. Thor must separately consider Terms (implied terms) or Misrepresentation (if there was an active false statement).

> **Exam trap**: "The other party knew of the mistake" ≠ Unilateral Mistake automatically made out. The mistake must be about a **term**. Mere expectation as to quality is often not a term.

</details>

## PART C · Unilateral Mistake — Mistaken Identity

---

### Scenario C1 — Face-to-Face, the Fraudster

Sif listed a precious Viking sword on Gumtree to sell. The fraudster Loki came in person and showed a forged ID saying, "I am Odin, the famous antiques collector." Trusting Odin's reputation, Sif handed over the sword in exchange for a $10,000 cheque. The cheque bounced. Loki sold the sword to Thor for $8,000, and Thor bought it in good faith.

**Q: Can Sif recover the sword from Thor?**

<details>
<summary>▶ Model Answer</summary>

**Issue**: Unilateral Mistake — Mistaken Identity, face-to-face

**Rule**:
- In a face-to-face contract there is a **presumption** that the contracting party intended to deal with the person in front of them (*Phillips v Brooks [1919]*)
- → even if there is a mistake, the contract is **voidable** (not void)
- **Voidable**: a good-faith third party (Thor) who acquires rights before rescission → the **third party is protected**

**Application**:
- Sif and Loki were **face-to-face** → the presumption applies
- Sif intended to deal with the person in front of her (in fact Loki) → the mistake was as to an **attribute (pretending to be Odin)**, not identity
- The contract is voidable. But Thor acquired in good faith before Sif could rescind
- → Thor's title is protected (an exception to nemo dat)

**Conclusion**: Sif may rescind the contract, but **Thor has already acquired in good faith**, so she **cannot** recover the sword. She can only claim damages against Loki for the tort of fraud.

> **Key point**: Face-to-face → voidable → good-faith third-party acquisition → original owner loses

</details>

---

### Scenario C2 — At a Distance, the Person Named in the Documents

Janet sells designer bags online. A fraudster named Enchantress sent an email in the name of the real celebrity "Thor Odinson" to order a bag, and also stole his credit information. Janet, having confirmed that Thor Odinson's credit was good, shipped the bag to Enchantress. Enchantress sold the bag to Balder, who bought it in good faith.

**Q: Can Janet recover the bag from Balder?**

<details>
<summary>▶ Model Answer</summary>

**Issue**: Unilateral Mistake — Mistaken Identity, contracting at a distance

**Rule**:
- Contracting at a distance: no presumption. The person named in the contract (the email) is the contracting party (*Shogun Finance v Hudson [2004]*, *Cundy v Lindsay [1878]*)
- → Janet intended to **contract with Thor Odinson** and never contracted with Enchantress
- → contract **void** (no agreement at all)
- If void, title never passed to Enchantress → Balder cannot acquire title either

**Application**:
- Email dealing → at a distance → no presumption
- Janet dealt after confirming Thor Odinson's credit → Thor Odinson's identity was the premise of the contract
- Contract void → Enchantress holds no title → Balder cannot acquire

**Conclusion**: Contract **void** → Janet **can recover the bag from Balder** (the nemo dat principle — title one does not have cannot be transferred).

> **Compared with Face-to-face C1**: face-to-face (voidable, third party protected) vs at a distance (void, third party not protected)

</details>

### Scenario C3 — A Non-Existent Company

Volstagg sent a letter in the name of "Asgard Premium Meats Pte Ltd" and bought $50,000 worth of premium food ingredients on credit from Donald. In fact, the company was a non-existent shell company. Seeing the name "Asgard Premium Meats," Donald thought it was a large company and supplied the goods. Volstagg sold the goods to Fandral, who acquired in good faith.

**Q: Can Donald recover the goods from Fandral?**

<details>
<summary>▶ Model Answer</summary>

**Issue**: Unilateral Mistake — Non-existing party

**Rule** (*King's Norton Metal Co v Edridge [1897]*):
- Where one contracts with a non-existent party: the contracting party is taken to have contracted with **the person who wrote the letter**
- → the mistake is not operative → contract **voidable** (not void)
- **Distinction from Cundy v Lindsay**: in Cundy a real, famous company was impersonated → contract void / in King's Norton the company did not exist at all → voidable

**Application**:
- "Asgard Premium Meats Pte Ltd" does not actually exist
- Donald contracted with Volstagg, who wrote the letter → he had no intention of contracting with a specific, real legal entity
- Contract voidable → Fandral acquired in good faith before rescission → Fandral protected

**Conclusion**: Contract **voidable**, but Fandral acquired in good faith → Donald **cannot recover**.

> **Distinction summary**: impersonating a real company (Cundy) = void / a shell company (King's Norton) = voidable

</details>

## PART D · Non Est Factum

---

### Scenario D1 — The Elderly Person with Poor Eyesight

Borson (80 years old, very poor eyesight) thought he had signed a document "gifting" his house to his daughter Hela. But Hela had secretly swapped the document, so it was in fact a contract to "sell" the house for $1. Borson was not wearing his glasses and could not check the contents.

**Q: Can Borson plead Non Est Factum?**

<details>
<summary>▶ Model Answer</summary>

**Issue**: Whether Non Est Factum is made out

**Rule** (*Saunders v Anglia Building Society [1971]*):
- Requirement ①: the signed document is **radically different** from what was intended
- Requirement ②: the signer is **not negligent**
- General principle: signature = bound by the contents (*L'Estrange v Graucob [1934]*)

**Application**:
- ① **Radical difference**: "gift" vs "$1 sale" — a gift (gratuitous) and a sale (for consideration) are legally different in nature. One can arguably say they are radically different ✓
- ② **Negligence**: signing without wearing glasses → his own carelessness. But there was also Hela's **active swapping of the document**, a fraudulent act
  - In *Saunders*, negligence was the reason NEF failed
  - Here, because of the fraudulent swap, Borson's negligence may not be fully attributable

**Conclusion**: ① can be satisfied, ② is uncertain — but considering Hela's fraud + Borson's vulnerable state (old age, poor eyesight), the court may decline to find negligence. **NEF may be made out**. Arguing **Duress/Undue Influence** in parallel makes a stronger case.

> **Exam strategy**: NEF has narrow requirements. Always consider arguing Duress and UI in parallel.

</details>

---

### Scenario D2 — Signing Without Reading

Steve signed a contract without reading it when joining a new gym membership. He later found that the contract contained a clause: "2-year commitment, $2,000 penalty for early termination." Steve pleads Non Est Factum, saying "I didn't know there was such a clause."

**Q: Is Steve's plea made out?**

<details>
<summary>▶ Model Answer</summary>

**Issue**: Non Est Factum — where a clause was not noticed

**Rule**:
- General principle (*L'Estrange v Graucob [1934]*): a signer is bound by the contents even if they did not read it
- NEF: ① a radically different document + ② no negligence → both requirements must be satisfied

**Application**:
- ① **Radical difference**: Steve signed a gym membership contract → it was the right document. He simply did not notice one clause → **not a radically different document** ✗
- ② **Negligence**: did not read before signing → clear **negligence** ✗

**Conclusion**: NEF **not made out**. Steve is bound by the commitment clause. The L'Estrange principle applies.

> **Exam key**: NEF applies only where "you thought it was a different kind of document." If you simply did not notice a clause in the same kind of document, NEF is unavailable.

</details>

## PART E · Composite · Advise Question

---

### Scenario E1 — Composite Scenario (Exam Style)

Dionysus contacted Sif by email and impersonated a purchasing officer of "Olympus Premium Wines Pte Ltd" (a real, famous company). Trusting that company's credit, Sif supplied $100,000 worth of wine on credit. Dionysus received the wine and sold it to Balder for $70,000. Balder is a good-faith buyer.

**Q: (a) What type of Mistake can Sif claim, and what is its effect? (b) Can she recover the wine from Balder?**

<details>
<summary>▶ Model Answer</summary>

**(a) Type of Mistake and effect**

**Issue**: Unilateral Mistake — Mistaken Identity, contracting at a distance

**Rule**: In contracting at a distance, the person named in the contract/correspondence is the contracting party. Where a real company is impersonated, Sif intended to contract with that real company → no contract with Dionysus → **void** (*Cundy v Lindsay [1878]*, *Shogun Finance v Hudson [2004]*).

**Application**:
- Email (at a distance) → no presumption
- "Olympus Premium Wines Pte Ltd" is a **real** company → Cundy applies (distinguished from King's Norton)
- Sif dealt by specifically relying on that company's credit → identity was the premise of the contract
- → contract **void**

**(b) Whether recovery from Balder is possible**

**Rule**: Contract void → title never passed to Dionysus → Balder cannot acquire title (nemo dat)

**Conclusion**: Contract **void** → **recovery of the wine from Balder is possible**. Sif may also separately claim damages in tort against Dionysus.

> **Distinguishing point**: impersonating a real company (void) vs a shell company (voidable) vs face-to-face fraud (voidable)

</details>

### Scenario E2 — Which Remedy to Choose?

Hela and Fandral agreed to buy and sell a particular piece of machinery for $500,000. At the time of contracting, both parties believed the equipment actually existed, but in fact it was a hypothetical model that existed only in the manufacturer's catalogue (no physical unit). This came to light after the contract.

**Q: Discuss the remedies Hela can claim, separately under Common Law and Equity. Which is more advantageous?**

<details>
<summary>▶ Model Answer</summary>

**Issue**: Common Mistake (akin to Res Extincta) — comparing CL and Equity remedies

**Common Law analysis**:
- The subject matter (the equipment) did not exist at the time of contracting → akin to Res Extincta
- Applying *Couturier v Hastie* → contract **void**
- If Fandral received the price, he must return it / Hela is under no obligation to perform
- Drawback: if void, all clauses are void → ancillary agreements are also lost

**Equity analysis** (*Chwee Kin Keong v Digilandmall [2005]*):
- If the mistake is "sufficiently serious," Equity treats the contract as **voidable**
- Rescission + conditional remedies are available (e.g. an order to compensate part of the costs)
- If a third party has acquired rights in good faith, the equitable remedy is unavailable

**Which is more advantageous?**
- For Hela, who has already paid the price → **CL void** is advantageous: full refund of the price + no obligation to perform
- If loss arose from partial performance, Equity's flexible remedies may be worth considering

**Conclusion**: Argue **CL (void)** primarily. If the court applies the CL mistake requirement (radically different) strictly, argue **Equity (voidable)** in the alternative.

> **Exam strategy**: "Equity is more flexible than CL, but where the effect of void is needed, CL is more powerful."

</details>

## Quick Issue-Spotting Checklist

When reading a scenario, check in the following order:

```
1. Are both parties mistaken?
   YES → Common or Mutual
   NO  → Unilateral (only one party mistaken)

2. Distinguish Common/Mutual:
   Same false assumption → Common Mistake
   Each thinking of something different → Mutual Mistake

3. Common Mistake details:
   Subject matter absent/perished → Res Extincta
   Already mine → Res Sua
   Quality/value difference → Broader Doctrine (Bell — very strict)

4. Unilateral Mistake details:
   Term mistake → actual knowledge → CL (void)
                  constructive knowledge + impropriety → Equity (voidable)
   Identity mistake → face-to-face → voidable (Phillips v Brooks)
                    → at a distance → void (Shogun / Cundy)
                    → shell company → voidable (King's Norton)

5. Document signing → consider Non Est Factum
   (radically different document + no negligence)

6. If a third party is involved, always:
   void → third party not protected
   voidable → good-faith acquisition before rescission → third party protected
```
