# Chapter 8 · Mistake — Quiz Cards

> Format: read **Q**, think of the answer, then check **A**. Effect: Void = contract never existed / Voidable = can be rescinded

---

## PART 1 · Overview & Structure

---

**Q1.** What is the legal effect of Mistake? How does it differ from Misrepresentation?

**A1.**
- Operative mistake → contract **void ab initio** (void from the start, no damages)
- Misrepresentation → contract **voidable** (can be rescinded + damages available)
- Mistake is a much narrower remedy: the requirements to render a contract void are very strict

---

**Q2.** List the 4 types of Mistake.

**A2.**
1. **Mutual Mistake** — the parties each have something different in mind (no agreement at all)
2. **Common Mistake** — the parties share the same false assumption
3. **Unilateral Mistake** — only one party is mistaken, and the other knew or should have known
4. **Non Est Factum** — the signed document is radically different from what was intended

---

## PART 2 · Mutual Mistake

---

**Q3.** What is Mutual Mistake? What is the effect on the contract?

**A3.**
- The parties **each have something different in mind** as to what is offered and what is accepted
- No point of agreement between Offer and Acceptance → **the contract is not formed at all (void)**
- Equity cannot intervene either (since there is no contract)

---

**Q4.** *Raffles v Wichelhaus [1864]* — what are the facts and the principle?

**A4.**
- **Facts**: a contract to ship cotton on the "Peerless" → in fact there were two ships called Peerless sailing from Liverpool (October, December). The claimant intended the October ship, the defendant the December ship
- **Principle**: the parties intended radically different subject matter → Mutual Mistake → **contract void**
- **Point**: the "Peerless case" — same word but entirely different subject matter

---

## PART 3 · Common Mistake

---

**Q5.** What is the definition of Common Mistake and its requirements?

**A5.**
- The parties conclude the contract **sharing the same false assumption**
- Requirement: the mistake must make the subject matter **fundamentally and radically different**
- Effect: Common law → **void** / Equity → **voidable**

---

**Q6.** What is Res Extincta? What are the relevant cases?

**A6.**
- **Res Extincta** = the subject matter has already perished/does not exist at the time of contracting
- *Couturier v Hastie [1856]*: corn already sold in transit → both parties unaware → **contract void**
- **Exception**: *McRae v Commonwealth [1951]* — where, by the structure of the contract, one party **assumed the risk** of non-existence of the subject matter → that party is liable for breach of contract

---

**Q7.** Explain the exception principle in *McRae v Commonwealth [1951]*.

**A7.**
- The Commonwealth falsely asserted the location of a sunken oil tanker → McRae contracted to salvage it
- The tanker did not exist
- **Principle**: the Commonwealth **warranted** the existence of the tanker → it assumed the risk
- → Res Extincta does not apply, the Commonwealth is liable for breach

---

**Q8.** What is Res Sua?

**A8.**
- **Res Sua** = a contract where the buyer unknowingly buys something he **already owns**
- Both parties mistakenly believe ownership lies elsewhere → **contract void**

---

**Q9.** Under the Broader Doctrine of Common Mistake (mistake as to quality), what is the Common Law requirement? What are the cases?

**A9.**
- *Bell v Lever Brothers [1932]*: a Common law mistake is **very narrow**
- Requirement: the mistake must make the subject matter **essentially and radically different**
- A mere mistake as to quality or value does not qualify
- *Bell* itself: Bell already had a terminable contract, yet the company paid a large sum → **mistake present but not void** (not essentially different)
- *Associated Japanese Bank v Credit du Nord [1989]*: the guaranteed machines did not exist → mistake found → void

---

**Q10.** How is Common Mistake treated under Equity?

**A10.**
- *Chwee Kin Keong v Digilandmall [2005]* (Singapore): retains the equitable doctrine of mistake
- In Equity, if the mistake is **sufficiently serious**, the contract is **voidable** (can be rescinded)
- A **more flexible** remedy than Common law is available
- Effect: voidable → a third party who acquires rights in good faith is protected

---

## PART 4 · Unilateral Mistake

---

**Q11.** What are the 2 sub-types of Unilateral Mistake?

**A11.**
1. **Mistake as to a term of contract** — applies in both CL & Equity
2. **Mistaken identity** — applies in **CL only**

---

**Q12.** What are the requirements for the Common Law remedy in a mistake as to a term?

**A12.**
Test from *Smith v Hughes [1871]* & *Chwee Kin Keong [2005]*:
- ① the mistake is as to a **fundamental term**
- ② the non-mistaken party **actually knew** of the mistake (actual knowledge)
→ contract **void** (CL)

**Note**: Caveat emptor (buyer-beware principle) — the seller has no duty to correct the buyer's self-induced misjudgement

---

**Q13.** What are the requirements for the Equity remedy in a mistake as to a term?

**A13.**
Test from *Chwee Kin Keong [2005]*:
- ① the mistake is as to a **material term**
- ② the non-mistaken party had **constructive knowledge** of the mistake
- ③ + unconscionable conduct (impropriety) / sharp practice
→ contract **voidable** (Equity, set aside)

---

**Q14.** Explain *Hartog v Colin & Shields [1939]*.

**A14.**
- **Facts**: the seller mistakenly quoted hare skins **"per pound"** instead of "per piece" (much cheaper)
- Industry practice was always per piece
- The buyer accepted despite knowing the obvious error
- **Principle**: the non-mistaken party (buyer) knew of the mistake → Unilateral Mistake (CL) → **contract void**

---

**Q15.** Mistaken identity — what is the principle in a face-to-face situation and the 2 cases?

**A15.**
- **Principle**: in a face-to-face contract there is a **presumption of intent to deal with the person present**
- → even if there is a mistake, the contract is, as a rule, **voidable** (mistake as to attribute, not identity)

**Case 1** — *Phillips v Brooks [1919]*:
- A fraudster posed as a nobleman, bought jewellery, paid by cheque, and pawned it
- Court: the seller intended to deal with **the fraudster himself** (mistake as to attribute rather than the other party's identity)
- → contract **voidable**, the pawnbroker protected as a good-faith acquirer

**Case 2** — *Ingram v Little [1961]*:
- Sisters were willing to sell a car only for a cash transaction. A fraudster impersonated a real person
- Court: the sisters made **that specific person's identity** a premise of the contract
- → **mistaken identity** found, contract **void** (exceptional result)

---

**Q16.** Mistaken identity — what is the principle in an at-a-distance situation and the case?

**A16.**
- **Principle**: at a distance there is no presumption. Intention is judged by objective interpretation of the documents/evidence

**Case** — *Shogun Finance v Hudson [2004]* (UK HL):
- A fraudster used another person's licence to buy a car on hire-purchase and sold it to Hudson
- The name on the contract = the licence holder (not the fraudster)
- Court: Shogun intended to contract with **the licence holder** → no contract with the fraudster
- → contract **void** → Hudson has no title

*Cundy v Lindsay [1878]*:
- A fraudster wrote to Lindsay posing as a famous company, bought linen on credit, and sold it to Cundy
- → at a distance, contract **void** → Cundy has no title

---

**Q17.** What happens where one contracts with a non-existing party?

**A17.**
- *King's Norton Metal Co v Edridge [1897]*:
- A fraudster sent a letter in the name of a company that did not actually exist
- Court: the claimant intended to contract with **the writer of the letter** → the mistake was not operative
- → contract **voidable** (not void)
- **Reason**: he did not deal by identifying a non-existent company, but dealt with the person who sent the letter

---

## PART 5 · Non Est Factum

---

**Q18.** What is Non Est Factum? What are the 2 requirements?

**A18.**
- **Literal meaning**: "It is not my deed"
- General principle: once you sign, you are bound even if you did not read (*L'Estrange v Graucob [1934]*)
- Requirements to make a contract **void** under the **Non Est Factum exception**:
  1. the signed document is **radically different** from the intended document
  2. the signer is **not negligent**

---

**Q19.** Explain *Saunders v Anglia Building Society [1971]*.

**A19.**
- **Facts**: an elderly woman signed a document while she had lost her glasses. It was a contract by which her nephew offered the house as loan security, but she believed it was a document transferring the house to her nephew
- Court: Non Est Factum **failed** — reasons:
  - the document concerned her house and was thus the same → **not radically different**
  - + she was **negligent** at the time of signing (signed without reading)
- **Principle**: NEF is applied extremely narrowly; if there is negligence, it fails

## PART 6 · Comparison & Synthesis

---

**Q20.** Void vs Voidable — explain the difference in effect on third parties.

**A20.**
| | **Void** | **Voidable** |
|---|---|---|
| **Meaning** | Contract never existed | Contract valid but can be rescinded |
| **Third party** | Title cannot pass — third party not protected | If a good-faith third party acquires rights before rescission, **protected** |
| **Applicable mistake** | Common law mistake, Non Est Factum | Equity mistake, face-to-face mistaken identity |

---

**Q21.** Classify the cases below by type.

| Case | Type | Effect |
|------|------|------|
| *Raffles v Wichelhaus* | Mutual Mistake | Void |
| *Couturier v Hastie* | Common — Res Extincta | Void |
| *Bell v Lever Bros* | Common — Quality (failed) | Valid |
| *Chwee Kin Keong* | Unilateral (term) Equity + Common Equity | Voidable |
| *Hartog v Colin & Shields* | Unilateral (term) CL | Void |
| *Phillips v Brooks* | Unilateral — Identity, face-to-face | Voidable |
| *Ingram v Little* | Unilateral — Identity, face-to-face (exception) | Void |
| *Shogun Finance v Hudson* | Unilateral — Identity, distance | Void |
| *Cundy v Lindsay* | Unilateral — Identity, distance | Void |
| *King's Norton v Edridge* | Unilateral — Non-existing party | Voidable |
| *Saunders v Anglia* | Non Est Factum (failed) | Valid |

---

**Q22.** Exam question — which Mistake type applies to the following scenario?

> A agrees to sell B "the goods in the warehouse." A intends Warehouse 1, B intends Warehouse 2. The contract simply states "the goods in the warehouse."

**A22.**
- **Mutual Mistake** — the parties intend different warehouses
- *Raffles v Wichelhaus* applies
- Disagreement on a fundamental aspect (which warehouse) → **contract void**
- Equity cannot intervene (there is no contract)

---

**Q23.** Exam question — which Mistake type applies to the following scenario?

> An online shopping mall mistakenly displays a $3,000 laptop as $30. 50 consumers place orders.

**A23.**
- **Unilateral Mistake as to a term** (price) — only the seller is mistaken; the consumers knew or should have known
- CL: *Chwee Kin Keong* — the extreme difference between the normal price ($3,000) and the displayed price ($30) → the buyer **knew or should have known** of the mistake
  - actual knowledge → **void** (CL)
  - constructive knowledge + unconscionable conduct → **voidable** (Equity)
- Singapore court: found mistake in Equity, contract set aside
