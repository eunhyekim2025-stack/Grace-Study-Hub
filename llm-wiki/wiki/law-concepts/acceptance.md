# Acceptance

> Applies only to bilateral contracts. Unilateral contracts require no notice of acceptance — see [[wiki/law-concepts/offer]].

## Rules of Acceptance (the 4 principles)

1. **Final & unqualified assent** — final and unconditional agreement to the terms of the offer → *Gay Choon Ing v Loh [2009]*
2. **Objective intention to be bound** → *Smith v Hughes [1871]*
3. **No counter-offer** — there must be no counter-offer
4. **Communication** — must be communicated to the offeror

---

## Communication of Acceptance

General principle: acceptance must be **communicated** to the offeror. Mere mental assent is insufficient.

### Conduct (acceptance by conduct)
Acceptance can be made by conduct even without express acceptance → *Brogden v Metropolitan Railway [1877]*

### Instantaneous Communication (phone / telex / fax / email / SMS)
- If the offeror does not specify a method of acceptance, an equivalent or faster method is also valid → *Manchester Diocesan Council v Commercial and General Investment [1970]*
- **Receipt rule**: the communication is effective only when it reaches the offeror → *Entores v Miles Far East [1955]*
- A voicemail within business hours: valid even if not listened to → *The Brimnes [1975]*
- **Invalid**: a telex outside business hours → *Brinkibon v Stahag Stahl [1983]*

### Postal Acceptance Rule (PAR)
Does not apply to instantaneous communication (*Entores*). Dispatch rule:
- Acceptance is complete at the moment the letter is posted → *Adams v Lindsell [1818]*
- Whether or not it arrives is irrelevant → *Household Fire Insurance v Grant [1879]*
- **2 conditions**: (1) postal acceptance is expressly/impliedly permitted, (2) the stamp and address are correct

### Silence
Silence cannot be a method of acceptance → *Felthouse v Bindley [1862]*
- Exception: where the offeree themselves has stated they will treat silence as acceptance → *Re Selectmove [1995]*

---

## Exceptions

- **Cross-Offers**: no meeting of minds → no contract → *Tinn v Hoffman [1873]*
- **Ignorance of Offer**: an offer one is unaware of cannot be accepted → *R v Clarke [1927]*
- **Battle of the Forms**: last shot wins → *Butler Machine Tool v Ex-Cell-O [1979]*

---

## Electronic Transactions Act (ETA)

See [[wiki/statutes/electronic-transactions-act]].
- **s.11(1)-(2)**: offer and acceptance may be made by electronic communication, and are not invalid merely for being electronic
- **s.13(1)**: time of dispatch = when it leaves a system under the originator's control
- **s.13(2)**: time of receipt = when it becomes capable of being retrieved at the designated address
- **s.14**: an electronic proposal addressed to the world at large = ITT (unless an intention to be bound is stated)
- **s.15**: contracts between automated systems are also valid
