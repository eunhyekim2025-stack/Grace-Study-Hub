# Offer

> The legal issue is whether (\_) has made an **offer** to (\_).

## Essentials of a Contract

| Element | Description |
|------|------|
| Consensus ad idem | "meeting of the minds" → Offer + Acceptance |
| [[wiki/law-concepts/consideration]] | Consideration |
| [[wiki/law-concepts/iclr]] | Intention to Create Legal Relations |
| [[wiki/law-concepts/capacity]] | Contractual capacity |
| Absence of vitiating factors | No factors that invalidate the contract |

## Rules of Offer (the 3 principles)

1. **Definite promise to be bound** — there must be a clear promise to be bound once accepted  
   → *Gay Choon Ing v Loh Sze Ti Terence Peter [2009] SGCA 3*
2. **Objective intention to be bound** — an intention to be bound must be established on an objective standard  
   → *Smith v Hughes [1871]*
3. **Communication** — the offer must be communicated to the offeree

---

## Invitation to Treat (ITT)

An ITT is an act inviting negotiation and carries no contractual binding force. A response to an ITT is treated as an **offer**.  
A reply to a mere request for information is not an acceptance → *Harvey v Facey [1893]*

### The 4 types of ITT

#### 1. Advertisement

| | Principle | Authority |
|--|------|------|
| **Principle** | Advertisement = ITT (seller's limited stock, possibility of further negotiation) | *Partridge v Crittenden [1968]* |
| **Exception** | If an intention to be bound is clearly shown, it is an offer | *Carlill v Carbolic Smoke Ball Co [1893]* — the £1,000 deposit proved the intention to be bound |

#### 2. Display of Goods

| | Principle | Authority |
|--|------|------|
| **Principle** | Display = ITT; the contract forms when payment is accepted at the till | *Fisher v Bell [1960]*, *Pharmaceutical Society v Boots Cash Chemists [1953]* |
| **Reason** | Protects the seller's right to refuse where there is a mispricing or stock shortage; the buyer too may change their mind after taking the item off the shelf | |

#### 3. Auction Sales

| | Principle | Authority |
|--|------|------|
| **Principle** | A call for bids = ITT; each bid = offer; acceptance occurs when the hammer falls | *Payne v Cave [1789]*, Sale of Goods Act s.57(2) |
| | An advertisement of an auction itself ≠ a promise to hold the auction | *Harris v Nickerson [1873]* |
| **Exception** | A "without reserve" advertisement = an offer to sell to the highest bidder (collateral contract) | *Warlow v Harrison [1859]* |

#### 4. Tenders

| | Principle | Authority |
|--|------|------|
| **Principle** | An invitation to tender = ITT; each tender = offer | *Spencer v Harding [1870]* |
| **Exception** | If there is a promise to accept the highest bid → a collateral contract arises | *Harvela Investments v Royal Trust Co of Canada [1986]* |

## Types of Contract

### Unilateral Contract
- An offer made to the world at large; promise ↔ **act**
- No notice of acceptance required
- Leading authority: *Carlill v Carbolic Smoke Ball Co [1893]*

### Bilateral Contract
- Between specific parties; promise ↔ **promise**
- Notice of acceptance required

---

## Termination of Offer

### 1. Withdrawal / Revocation

- **Principle**: An offer may be revoked at any time before acceptance, even if a time limit was promised  
  → *Dickinson v Dodds [1876]*, *Routledge v Grant [1828]*
- **Exception (Firm Offer)**: A promise to keep an offer open is binding if supported by consideration  
  → *Mountford v Scott [1975]*
- **Communication of revocation**: A revocation is only effective once communicated to the offeree  
  → *Byrne & Co v Van Tienhoven & Co [1880]*
  - Effective on receipt by a communication device within business hours (actual reading not required) → *The Brimnes [1975]*
  - Notice through a reliable third party is also effective → *Dickinson v Dodds [1876]*

**Special rule for unilateral offers**: An offer cannot be revoked once the offeree has begun performance  
→ *Daulia v Four Millbank [1978]*  
(However, if performance is not completed, the offeree cannot claim against the offeror)

### 2. Rejection / Counter-Offer

- A counter-offer = rejection of the original offer + a new offer  
  → *Hyde v Wrench [1840]* — the original offer cannot be accepted after a counter-offer
- A mere **request for information** is not a counter-offer → *Stevenson v McLean [1880]*

### 3. Lapse of Time

- Where a time limit is stated: no acceptance after the time has passed
- Where no time limit is stated: lapses after a reasonable time has passed (judged on the circumstances)  
  → *Ramsgate Victoria Hotel Co Ltd v Montefiore [1866]*

### 4. Failure of Condition

- Where the offer is subject to a particular condition, it cannot be accepted if the condition is not met  
  → *Financings Ltd v Stimson [1962]* — where the state of the subject matter had materially changed

### 5. Death

| Situation | Result |
|------|------|
| Offeror dies, offeree **knows** | Offer lapses |
| Offeror dies, offeree **does not know** | Acceptance is valid → *Bradbury v Morgan [1862]* |
| (Except contracts for personal services) | |
| **Offeree** dies | Offer lapses, and the heirs cannot accept → *Reynolds v Atherton [1921]* |
