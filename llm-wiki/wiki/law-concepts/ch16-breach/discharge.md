# Discharge of Contract

The four ways contractual obligations are extinguished: **performance · agreement · breach · frustration**.

## 1. Discharge by Performance
Principle: **entire performance** is required → *Cutter v Powell [1795]*
- Exceptions (partial performance recognised): (a) severable contract, (b) substantial performance → *Hoenig v Isaacs [1952]*, (c) acceptance of partial performance, (d) prevention of performance, (e) acceptance of voluntary performance

## 2. Discharge by Agreement
- Where both parties have outstanding obligations, mutual release (bilateral discharge)
- Where only one party has an outstanding obligation, separate consideration or a deed is required (accord and satisfaction)

## 3. Discharge by Breach
A breach does not automatically terminate — **the innocent party elects** (to terminate or affirm).

### When a right to terminate arises (*RDC Concrete v Sato Kogyo [2007] SGCA* — the 4 situations)
| Situation | Content |
|------|------|
| **Situation 1** | The contract states a right to terminate on breach |
| **Situation 2** | The breaching party renounces performance (renunciation) — repudiatory |
| **Situation 3(a)** | Breach of a **condition** (the nature of the term) |
| **Situation 3(b)** | Breach of an **innominate term** + the consequences are serious (deprive substantially the whole benefit) → *Hong Kong Fir [1962]* |

- **Anticipatory breach**: immediate termination / claim available → *Hochster v De La Tour [1853]*
- On electing to terminate: future obligations are extinguished + damages; on electing to affirm, the risk is borne → *White & Carter v McGregor [1962]*

## 4. Discharge by Frustration
See [[wiki/law-concepts/frustration]] — automatic termination due to a supervening event.
