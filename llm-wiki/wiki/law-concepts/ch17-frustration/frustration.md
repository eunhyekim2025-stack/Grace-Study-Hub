# Frustration

After a contract is formed, a **supervening event** without fault of the parties makes performance impossible, illegal or fundamentally different → the contract is **automatically discharged**.

## Grounds

| Type | Description | Authority |
|------|------|------|
| **Supervening impossibility** | Destruction of the subject matter / death or illness of a specific person | *Taylor v Caldwell [1863]* |
| **Supervening illegality** | Subsequent illegality (war, change in the law) | *Fibrosa v Fairbairn [1943]* |
| **Radical change in circumstances** | Complete disappearance of the purpose of the contract | *Krell v Henry [1903]* (the coronation case) |
| (Distinguish) Mere hardship / increased cost does not suffice | | *Davis Contractors v Fareham [1956]* |

## Limits (frustration denied)

- **Self-induced frustration**: caused by a party's own act → not frustrated → *Maritime National Fish v Ocean Trawlers [1935]*
- **Foreseeable / provided for**: foreseeable, or provided for in the contract (force majeure) → not frustrated
- Mere difficulty / uneconomic performance → not frustrated

## Singapore application
- *Glahe International v ACS Computer [1999]*, *Lim Kim Som v Sheriffa Taibah [1994]* — the radical change test

---

## Effects — Frustrated Contracts Act (FCA)
See [[wiki/statutes/frustrated-contracts-act]]
- **s.2(2)**: money paid before discharge is recoverable, and obligations to pay are extinguished
- **s.2(3)**: expenses incurred by the other party may be deducted at the court's discretion
- **s.2(4)**: a reasonable sum is payable for any valuable benefit one party received
