# Exemption Clause

A clause that excludes (exclusion) or limits (limitation) liability. **3-stage analysis**: incorporation → construction → statutory control.

## 1. Incorporation

| Method | Principle | Authority |
|------|------|------|
| **Signature** | A signatory is bound even without reading | *L'Estrange v Graucob [1934]* |
| | Exception: where induced by misrepresentation | *Curtis v Chemical Cleaning [1951]* |
| **Notice** | Reasonable notice required **before** the moment of contracting | *Olley v Marlborough Court [1949]*, *Thornton v Shoe Lane Parking [1971]* |
| | The more unusual the clause, the clearer the notice required | *Spurling v Bradshaw [1956]* (red hand rule) |
| **Course of Dealing** | Incorporated through a consistent past course of dealing | *McCutcheon v David MacBrayne [1964]* |

## 2. Construction

- **Contra proferentem rule**: ambiguities are construed against the drafter
- Excluding negligence: clear wording required → *Canada Steamship Lines v The King [1952]*

## 3. Statutory Control

### Unfair Contract Terms Act (UCTA)
See [[wiki/statutes/unfair-contract-terms-act]]
- **s.2(1)**: liability for **death or personal injury** caused by negligence can never be excluded
- **s.2(2)**: excluding other negligence loss is permitted only if it satisfies the **reasonableness test**
- **s.3**: excluding liability for breach in standard-form / consumer contracts is also subject to the reasonableness test
- **s.11 + Schedule 2**: factors for assessing reasonableness (bargaining power, alternatives, awareness, etc.)

### Consumer Protection (Fair Trading) Act (CPFTA)
[[wiki/statutes/consumer-protection-act]] — protects consumers from unfair practices
