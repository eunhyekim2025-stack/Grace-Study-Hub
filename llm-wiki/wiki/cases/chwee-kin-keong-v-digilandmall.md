# Chwee Kin Keong v Digilandmall.com Pte Ltd [2005] SGCA 2 (HC [2004] SGHC 71)

> **Court below (HC)**: V K Rajah JC · judgment 2004-04-12 · [2004] SGHC 71
> **Appeal (CA)**: Chao Hick Tin JA, Kan Ting Chiu J, Yong Pung How CJ · judgment 2005-01-13 · judgment authored by Chao Hick Tin JA · amicus curiae Assoc Prof Daniel Seng
> Singapore's leading case on **online price mistake**. It squarely addresses the common law/equity divide in **unilateral mistake**, **offer and acceptance in internet contracts**, and the doctrine of **snapping up**.

## One-Line Summary

When an HP colour laser printer priced at S$3,854 was posted at **S$66** due to an employee's data-entry error, six plaintiffs who knew (or ought to have known) of the error ordered a total of 1,606 units (the first appellant 100 units, the third appellant 760 units) and sued to enforce the contract. **HC: because the plaintiffs had actual or constructive knowledge of the price error, the contracts were void for unilateral mistake.** **CA: common law unilateral mistake requires *actual knowledge* (constructive knowledge is insufficient), but where there is *constructive knowledge + unconscionable conduct*, equity may intervene to set the contract aside — accordingly the appeal was dismissed (with only the costs portion partly varied).**

## Facts [HC para 4–10; CA para 4–8]

- The defendant Digilandmall sold IT products over the internet, operating three sites: the D Website, the HP Website (buyhp.com.sg), and the corporate website of its associate DIL.
- On the afternoon of 2003-01-08, a DIL employee, **Samuel Teo**, mistakenly uploaded a "Price Mass Upload" training template (created with the real product number HP 9660A plus a fabricated price) to the live site. As a result the price changed from S$3,854 to **S$66**, and the product description was simultaneously changed to the number **"55"**, taking immediate effect across all three sites. Samuel Teo had neither the authority nor the intention to change the price, and it was undisputed that the error was *bona fide*. [HC para 6–9]
- At about 01:17 in the early hours of 2003-01-13, the first appellant (Chwee Kin Keong, call-name "Scorpio") became aware of the error during an ICQ chat with his friend Desmond. Desmond stated "I think one of the wrong posted price", with the normal price being "3 to 4k outside / USD 2k". Scorpio replied "damn don't tell me they realised their error already", "sell them cheap … shd buy more", and that if they did not honour it, "sue them lor". [HC para 15; CA para 10–11]
- At 02:58 the first appellant sent a mass email to **54** friends and business associates: "IMPT – HP Colour LaserJet going at only $66!!", stating *"I do not know if this is an error … No harm trying right? … coz they might change it anytime."* [HC para 18; CA para 12]
- The six plaintiffs placed large orders within a short time (the 1st and 5th 100 units each, the 3rd 760 units + 330 units in his brother's name). In total, 784 persons placed 1,008 orders for 4,086 units. [HC para 10; CA para 8]
- Around 09:15 the defendant discovered the error after a customer enquiry → it immediately removed the product from the sites. On 2003-01-14 it gave notice of non-fulfilment. [HC para 76; CA para 7]
- The automated system sent a "Successful Purchase Confirmation" email automatically for each order. The availability field stated "call to enquire". [HC para 72–75]

## Issues

1. Did a *prima facie* contract form through internet offer and acceptance, and was the automated reply email an acceptance? [HC para 91–101, 134–138]
2. Was there consideration? [HC para 139]
3. Did the plaintiffs **know** of the price error (actual/constructive knowledge)? [HC para 140–148; CA para 88–97]
4. Does **unilateral mistake** render the contract void at common law — and is *constructive* knowledge sufficient, or is *actual* knowledge required? [HC para 104–149; CA para 30–55]
5. Is there an **equitable rescission** jurisdiction over unilateral mistake? [CA para 56–83]

## Court Below (High Court, [2004] SGHC 71)

- **Internet contracts**: ordinary contract-law principles apply but require modification to suit the features of the internet. [HC para 91]
- **[[statutes/electronic-transactions-act|Electronic Transactions Act (ETA)]]**: s 11 permits offer and acceptance electronically; s 13 deems messages from an automated system to originate from the party; s 13(8) preserves agency and contract-formation law; s 15 deals only with procedure (time of receipt) and does not change the substantive law. [HC para 92, 99]
- **Nature of web advertising**: in principle an invitation to treat. But while limited stock is the basis for treating a physical store's display as an invitation to treat, on the internet stock availability is unknown, so unless the merchant states conditions such as "while stocks last" there is a risk it will be construed as an offer of unlimited supply — **the merchant bears the responsibility of protecting itself**. [HC para 93–96]
- **Automated reply = acceptance**: the "Successful Purchase Confirmation" email was a clear acceptance. "call to enquire" related to the **time of delivery** (not stock) → it was not a conditional condition precedent. [HC para 136–138]
- **Consideration**: modern contract law finds consideration very readily. Mutual promises — the promise to pay the price ↔ the promise to deliver the printer — suffice on their own. [HC para 139]
- **Unilateral mistake / "snapping up"**: the objective theory is the rule, but there is an exception where the other party *knows* that the offeror's true intention is not the natural meaning (*Hartog v Colin & Shields*). The core is the absence of *consensus ad idem*. [HC para 104–116]
- **Constructive knowledge suffices**: Rajah JC held (relying on Canada, *OT Africa*, *Ho Seng Lee*) that not only actual knowledge but also **constructive (deemed) knowledge** triggers common law unilateral mistake. Where there is a "real reason" to raise suspicion and a party turns a blind eye (Nelsonian/wilful blindness), knowledge is deemed. [HC para 109–113]
- **Equity**: extending *Great Peace Shipping* (a common-mistake case) to unilateral mistake, no separate equitable jurisdiction over unilateral mistake was retained where the contract was void at common law (law reform = a matter for the legislature; cf. NZ Contractual Mistakes Act 1977). [HC para 121–133]
- **Findings of fact**: all six actually knew the price was an error, and in the alternative constructive knowledge was also found. The contracts were void ab initio. Claims dismissed, costs entirely against the defendant. [HC para 140–156]

## Holding

CA: **Appeal dismissed** (though the first-instance costs, originally entirely against the defendant, were varied so the appellants bear only *two-thirds*, because the defendant lost on many issues). The findings of fact on each appellant's knowledge of the price error were upheld, and the contracts were void for unilateral mistake (or capable of being set aside in equity). [CA para 88–99, 104–107]

## Law (Ratio / Core Doctrine)

### 1. Formation of internet contracts — offer, acceptance, ETA [HC para 91–101, 134–138]
- Traditional offer/acceptance and *consensus ad idem* principles apply to the internet, taking account of differences in stock and delivery mechanisms. **To keep a web posting as an invitation to treat, the merchant must state availability and any conditions precedent**, and must manage automated replies so they objectively reflect its intention. [HC para 94–96]
- [[law-concepts/part1-formation/offer]] and [[law-concepts/part1-formation/acceptance]]: an automated system's confirmation email can objectively be an acceptance (ETA s 13), and the person who wrote/programmed it is responsible. [HC para 136]
- The formation rules applicable to email vs web transactions (postal rule vs receipt rule) are discussed only by way of *obiter* — since web transactions are near-instantaneous and bilateral, the **receipt rule** may be the logical default, but this was not decided. [HC para 97–101]

### 2. Common law requirement for unilateral mistake — *actual knowledge* essential (the CA's key correction) [CA para 30–55]
- Principle: contract formation is judged on objective facts; motive and true intent are immaterial (promoting commercial certainty). [CA para 30]
- Exception: where the other party **knows** the offeror's true intention differs from the natural meaning, there is no consensus ad idem → the contract is void. The *locus classicus* = *Hartog v Colin & Shields*; *Smith v Hughes*. [CA para 31–35]
- **The CA's correction**: common law unilateral mistake is made out **only where there is *actual* knowledge**. Constructive knowledge is essentially a concept of *equity*, so Rajah JC's position of importing it into common law to make a contract void was wrong — conflating the two leaves no room for equity to intervene. [CA para 46, 51–53, 63]
- However, "actual knowledge" may be **inferred from all the circumstances** even without direct evidence, and deliberate disregard (**Nelsonian knowledge / wilful blindness**) also amounts to actual knowledge. A duty to inquire arises when there is a "real reason to suppose the existence of a mistake". [CA para 41–44]
- A contract void at common law is **void ab initio** — even a bona fide third party cannot acquire rights. (The essential difference from voidable in equity.) [CA para 46]

### 3. The *existence* of an equitable jurisdiction over unilateral mistake [CA para 56–83]
- **The CA affirmed the existence of an equitable jurisdiction over unilateral mistake** (expressly reversing the part the HC had denied). It takes the unconscionability basis of *Solle v Butcher* (Denning LJ) as a starting point, but *Great Peace Shipping*, being a *common*-mistake case, does not apply directly to unilateral mistake. [CA para 56–62, 74]
- **Requirements**: the non-mistaken party must have **constructive knowledge + additional impropriety** — i.e. there must be "sharp practice" or "unconscionable conduct" such as deliberately failing to point out a known possibility of mistake. **Constructive knowledge (or negligence) alone is insufficient** (accepting the position of amicus curiae Daniel Seng). Negligence per se does not trigger equity. [CA para 77–80]
- The unconscionability must be based on facts the non-mistaken party *actually knows*, and cannot be based on what a reasonable person would have known (objective imputation) (*Can-Dive*). [CA para 78]
- Equitable relief is **voidable** and therefore more flexible and able to **protect the rights of a bona fide third party**, making it more consonant with justice than common law void. [CA para 82–83]

### 4. "Snapping up" [HC para 115–117, 145–148; CA para 44]
- Derived from *Tamplin v James* (James LJ). Snapping up, with **indecent haste**, an offer one knows (or ought to know) is plainly mistaken, so as to take advantage — rushing before the error is corrected is the typical hallmark. [HC para 116]
- Expressions like "snapping up" are not themselves an independent doctrine, but merely a **basis for the court to infer the non-mistaken party's (actual) knowledge**. [CA para 44]
- Where the price is *absurdly low* relative to the market price (here S$66 vs S$3,854), a reasonable person should be suspicious and investigate, and ordering large quantities in haste nonetheless supports an inference of knowledge and sharp practice. [HC para 143–148]

### 5. Ancillary — consideration and procedure [HC para 139; CA para 100–106]
- Consideration: mutual promises alone suffice; modern law finds consideration readily (citing *Williams v Roffey*). → connects to the [[law-concepts/part1-formation/consideration]] discussion in [[cases/teo-seng-kee-bob-v-arianecorp]] and [[cases/gay-choon-ing-v-loh]]. [HC para 139]
- Amendment of pleadings (O 20 r 5(1)): an amendment formalising an issue already canvassed in evidence and at trial is permitted even after the close of proceedings, absent undue prejudice or surprise to the other party. [CA para 100–103]

## Exam Points / Significance

- **Key HC vs CA difference**: the HC found common law void on *constructive knowledge* alone, but the **CA held that common law requires *actual knowledge* only (constructive knowledge engages only equity), and that equity is triggered only by *constructive knowledge + impropriety***. The key in an answer is to apply the "two stages (common law / equity)" separately.
- **Five-degree fusion spectrum**: a case analysed in Assoc Prof Yeo Tiong Min, "Five Degrees of Fusion of Common Law and Equity" ([2004] SJLS 1) — citable in policy discussion (certainty vs justice). [CA para 81]
- **online contract**: a web advertisement is in principle an invitation to treat, but if the merchant does not state conditions it may be construed as an offer → automated reply = acceptance. [[statutes/electronic-transactions-act]] ss 11, 13 apply.
- The third-party-protection difference between **void ab initio (common law) vs voidable (equity)** is ultimately important (no third party here).
- **snapping up**: when the price is unreasonably low relative to the market, a buyer's indecent haste and bulk orders strongly support an inference of knowledge.
- Related cases: *Hartog v Colin & Shields* (locus classicus), *Great Peace Shipping* (common mistake, equitable jurisdiction denied — inapplicable to unilateral mistake), *Solle v Butcher*, *Shogun Finance v Hudson* (void ab initio, third parties), *Smith v Hughes*.
