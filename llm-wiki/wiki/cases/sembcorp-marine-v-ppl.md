# Sembcorp Marine Ltd v PPL Holdings Pte Ltd and another and another appeal [2013] SGCA 43

> **Court**: Court of Appeal (Sundaresh Menon CJ, Chao Hick Tin JA, Judith Prakash J) · **Decision**: 2013-07-25 · Judgment by: Menon CJ
> Singapore's leading case on **implied terms in fact**. It formulated a **three-step process** combining the **business efficacy** and **officious bystander** tests, and precisely distinguished the concepts of interpretation, implication, and construction.

## One-line Holding

An implied term gives effect to the parties' **presumed intention** to **fill a gap**, and is determined through a three-step process: **(1) how the gap arose (is it a true gap the parties did not contemplate?) → (2) whether implication is necessary for business efficacy → (3) a specific term to which the officious bystander, if asked, would respond "Oh, of course!"**. The reasonableness/interpretive approach of *Belize* is not Singapore law, and the standard remains **necessity**. [para 93–101]

## Facts [para 4–16]

- Sembcorp (oil rig and shipbuilding) and **PPL Holdings** (PPLH; operator of PPL Shipyard) formed a joint venture prompted by the Santa Fe project. Under a 2001 SPA, Sembcorp acquired a 50% stake in PPL Shipyard and the parties entered a **JVA (joint venture agreement)** — Sembcorp and PPLH (+E-Interface) each held 50%, with an **equal structure** including a **3:3 board, 3 votes for each party, and a quorum requiring at least 1 person from each party**. [para 4–7]
- In 2003, a **Supplemental Agreement (SA)** gave Sembcorp an additional 35% → Sembcorp 85% : PPLH (+E-Interface) 15%. However, the **JVA (particularly cll 5.1–5.8) and the articles were not amended**, and although Sembcorp appointed 3 additional directors (6:3), operations continued without dispute. [para 9–11]
- In 2010, when PPLH's parent Baker received an acquisition offer from Yangzijiang (director Aurol having leaked the 2009 accounts), Sembcorp tried to exercise control of the board — quorum and voting disputes (such as the 28 April 2010 resolution) erupted. [para 12–16]

## Court Below [para 20–21]

- **High Court judge**: Rejected all the implied terms Sembcorp asserted (in particular the **Equality Premise Clause** — the implication that the equal-stake premise clauses cease to apply once the stakes are no longer equal). There was also no breach of the confidentiality clause (cl 13) (the 2009 accounts had already lost confidentiality by the time they were disclosed). Adopted the *Belize* position in deciding the implied-terms question. [para 20, 24]
- PPLH counterclaim: the 28 April 2010 resolution and Aurol's removal were void for lack of quorum, but relief under s 216 (minority oppression) and s 216A was denied. [para 21]

## Issues [para 22–23]

1. Whether the **Equality Premise Clause** (implied into the JVA and articles) should be recognised — application of the implied-terms doctrine. [para 22]
2. Whether the confidentiality clause was breached and termination was justified.
3. (Ancillary) The admissibility of extrinsic evidence and the regulation of pleading and evidentiary procedure.

## Holding

CA 75 (Sembcorp's appeal) **partly allowed** — the Equality Premise Clause (in a reformulated, limited form) was **implied** into the JVA and articles: once a party loses its 50% stake, cll 5.2, 5.3, 5.5–5.8 (clauses parasitic on cl 5.1) no longer apply. The confidentiality-clause claim was dismissed. CA 77 (PPLH's appeal) **dismissed**. [para 128, 170]

## Ratio / Core Doctrine

### 1. Distinguishing the terms — interpretation · implication · construction [para 27–33]
- **Interpretation**: the process of attributing meaning to *an expression that exists* in the contract. Where there is no expression, there is nothing to interpret. [para 27–28]
- **Implication**: the process of filling the contract's *silence (a gap)* — inserting a term, in accordance with the parties' *presumed intention*, where there is no expression. An "ad hoc gap filler". [para 29, 81]
- **Construction**: a composite process encompassing both (+ rectification) — viewing the document *as a whole* to ascertain the parties' intention. [para 31]
- Implication is a species of *interpretation in the broad sense*, but it has **distinct tests and techniques**, so the two must not be confused (reaffirming *Foo Jong Peng*). [para 78, 81]

### 2. Rejection of *Belize* — the standard is necessity, not reasonableness [para 76–82, 98]
- The **reasonableness standard** proposed in Lord Hoffmann's *Belize*, and its approach of subsuming implication within interpretation, **is not Singapore law** — reaffirming the position in *Foo Jong Peng*. [para 76, 82]
- The standard for implication is **necessity** — reasonableness is a necessary but not sufficient condition. [para 82, 100]
- To the extent *Belize* spoke of "construction as a whole" it can be reconciled, but its reasonableness proposition is rejected. Rectification is not absorbed into contextual interpretation and retains an independent role. [para 79, 97]

### 3. Complementary combination of the business efficacy and officious bystander tests [para 83–92]
- **Business efficacy test** (*The Moorcock*): identifies the **existence of a gap (lacuna)** that must be filled for the contract to work commercially — but because what counts as the "minimum of efficacy" is ambiguous, it *points to the gap but does not determine which term should fill it*. [para 84, 86, 91]
- **Officious bystander test** (*Shirlaw*): **defines the specific term** that fills that gap — the term to which the parties would instantly respond "Oh, of course!". [para 88, 91]
- The two are not *opposed* but **complementary**: the officious bystander operates within the normative framework of *the necessity for business efficacy*. A term is implied **only where, out of necessity for business efficacy, the parties would immediately accept the proposed term**. [para 90, 92]

### 4. The three-step process (core) [para 93–101]
Implication is determined through the following **three-step process** [para 101]:
- **(1) How the gap arose**: ascertain how the gap came about. Implication is considered **only where the parties *did not contemplate* the gap**. (If the parties contemplated it but *mistakenly believed* the express terms were adequate (scenario b → subject to rectification), or failed to agree and *left it open* (scenario c), implication is unavailable.) [para 94–96, 101(a)]
- **(2) Necessity**: whether implying the term is **necessary in a business/commercial sense** to give the contract as a whole efficacy. [para 101(b)]
- **(3) Specific term**: whether, in light of the necessity for business efficacy, it is a specific term to which the parties would respond **"Oh, of course!"** if the officious bystander asked. If there is no such clear answer, the **gap remains and the parties bear the consequences**. [para 101(c)]
- **The threshold for implication remains high (necessary)** — this formulation does not change the existing high standard. *BP Refinery*'s additional requirements (reasonable, equitable, clearly expressed, not contradicting express terms) are in substance a restatement of the above principle. [para 98–100]

### 5. The relevant time for assessing implication [para 95, 127]
- Presumed intention is assessed against the factual matrix **at the time of contracting**. Even if the parties later *misunderstood* the legal effect of the SA and continued operating as before, this does not bar recognising an implied term that contradicts that misunderstanding. Subsequent conduct is relevant only when invoking waiver or estoppel. [para 95, 127]

### 6. Supplementing the admissibility of extrinsic evidence — pleading and evidentiary requirements [para 34–75]
- **Reaffirmed** the contextual approach of [[cases/zurich-v-b-gold|Zurich Insurance]] and its embedding in s 94(f). However, it emphasised the distinction that the [[statutes/evidence-act|Evidence Act]] governs only the *admissibility* of evidence and not the *substantive* rules of interpretation. [para 40–45]
- The **"robust approach"** (the NZ model — broadly admitting prior negotiations and subsequent conduct) *cannot be wholly adopted* given the limits of EA ss 95–96 and the adversarial nature of common law litigation procedure. [para 36–38, 65–72]
- It therefore introduced **four pleading requirements (civil procedure)** [para 73]: (1) *specifically plead* each fact of the factual matrix, (2) plead the circumstances showing that the fact was known to all parties, (3) state the effect the fact would have on the contended interpretation, (4) limit discovery to the relevance of the pleaded facts. — This is consistent with the limits in *Zurich* ([132(d)]) and controls the "tsunami of evidence". [para 73–74]

### 7. Application to the present case [para 102–128]
- Cl 5.1, with its proviso "so long as [holding 50% stake]", means that **once the stakes are no longer equal, neither party has the right to appoint 3 directors** (cl 5.1 itself already provides that result → no implication needed here). [para 114–116]
- Cll 5.2, 5.3, 5.5–5.8 are **parasitic** on cl 5.1 — they are silent on board operation in the situation where a party has lost its appointment right. This is a true, uncontemplated gap (1), implication is necessary for efficacy (2), and the officious bystander, if asked, would answer "of course they cease to apply" (3). → The implication is recognised that **once a party loses 50%, these clauses also cease to apply**. [para 117–128]

## Exam Points / Significance

- **Standard skeleton for an implied-terms answer = the three-step process ([para 101])**: how the gap arose (uncontemplated) → necessity for business efficacy → officious bystander "Oh, of course!". This decision is named in the implied-terms table of the wiki [[law-concepts/ch10-terms/terms]].
- Of the **three gap types** (uncontemplated / mistaken belief that express terms suffice / left open by failure to agree), **only (1) qualifies for implication** — scenario b is rectification, scenario c leaves the gap. [para 94–96]
- Rejection of *Belize* = the standard is **necessity** (not reasonableness). The **division of roles and complementarity** between business efficacy (identifying the gap) and officious bystander (defining the term) is key. [para 91]
- Using the **conceptual distinction** between interpretation, implication, and construction accurately is a high-scoring point. [para 27–33]
- Reaffirms the contextual approach of [[cases/zurich-v-b-gold]] but adds procedural discipline through the **four pleading requirements** — cited together on the extrinsic-evidence issue. [para 73]
- On the termination issue, it applied the four-situation taxonomy of [[cases/rdc-concrete-v-sato-kogyo|RDC Concrete]] to hold that the breach of cl 13.2 did not justify termination ([para 137–138]).
