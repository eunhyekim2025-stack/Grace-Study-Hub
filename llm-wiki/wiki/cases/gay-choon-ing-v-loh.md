# Gay Choon Ing v Loh Sze Ti Terence Peter [2009] SGCA 3

> **Court**: Court of Appeal (Chao Hick Tin JA, Andrew Phang Boon Leong JA, V K Rajah JA) · **Judgment**: 2009-01-08 · Judgment authored by: Phang JA
> A leading Singapore case on **compromise (settlement)**, famous for its *coda* squarely examining the **doctrine of consideration**.

## One-Line Summary

The simultaneously executed **Points of Agreement (POA)** and **Waiver Letter**, construed objectively in the context of the parties' correspondence, constitute a **valid compromise** terminating all existing disputes — accordingly reversing the court below, which had found breach of trust and rescission of the POA. [para 77, 89–90]

## Facts [para 12–36]

- The plaintiff (Loh) and the defendant (Gay) had been friends of 30 years since their Singapore Armed Forces days in the 1970s. The plaintiff was MD of the Kenyan company ASP; the defendant worked at ASP and obtained investment from the plaintiff's family company (Gay Lip Seng & Sons, "the Company", which ran the Tai Hoe Hotel).
- In 1993, for a hotel redevelopment, the defendant received about **S$1.4–1.55m** from the plaintiff and issued shares in the Company. Its character was disputed as **investment (plaintiff's case) vs loan (defendant's case)**. In 1994 a **Trust Deed** was executed — the defendant holding the shares on trust for the plaintiff.
- In 2003–2004, relations soured over the defendant's retirement from ASP and his severance, with many emotional letters exchanged. Both sides repeatedly expressed a wish to resolve matters **amicably**.
- On 2004-10-27 the **POA** was executed (the plaintiff selling 27.5% of the trust shares to the defendant for S$1.5m, payable in instalments) + **on the same day** the plaintiff, as ASP's representative, signed the **Waiver Letter** (the defendant leaving the Company with no claims against it, and the Company with no claims against him). On 2005-01-10 the full S$1.5m was paid and receipt acknowledged.
- Disputes later resurfaced over an alleged threat at a Shanghai dinner, and the plaintiff sued, raising criminal breach of trust.

## Court Below (High Court, [2008] SGHC 31) [para 4–10]

- Found that the defendant **held the shares on trust** for the plaintiff, **rescinded the POA** for breach of the **fair dealing rule**, but on condition that the plaintiff return the S$1.5m.
- The compromise argument was treated lightly as the defendant's "fallback argument", and the court found there was **no valid compromise** [para 9, 76]. → the appellate court elevated this issue to the heart of the case and reversed.

## Issues [para 37–40]

The true focus was not the trust/rescission issues the parties had contested but **whether the POA + Waiver Letter constitute a valid compromise**. If a compromise was reached, the trust obligations were extinguished by a "clean break". [para 39]

## Holding

CA 33/2008 (defendant's appeal) **allowed**; CA 34/2008 (plaintiff's appeal) **dismissed**. A valid compromise was made out → while the court below's careful findings on the remaining issues (trust/rescission) were correct, the compromise subsumes all of them. [para 89–91]

## Law (Ratio / Core Doctrine)

### 1. Compromise / Settlement [para 41–60]
- Compromise = "the termination of a dispute by mutual concessions" — in essence a **contract**, to which the ordinary contract-law requirements (agreement, consideration, intention to create legal relations) apply directly. An *actual or potential dispute* is a precondition. [para 43–46]
- Whether one is formed is judged **objectively**, looking at the **whole course of negotiations** to see whether agreement crystallised at a particular point. The mere fact that negotiations continued thereafter does not invalidate an agreement already formed. [para 53]

### 2. The objective approach — offer/acceptance, but non-mechanical [para 47–63]
- The traditional tools of contract formation are [[law-concepts/part1-formation/offer]] and [[law-concepts/part1-formation/acceptance]], but they must be applied *less mechanically*, with regard to **context**. Lord Denning's radical approach (*Butler Machine Tool*) is not adopted, but even the traditional approach must always keep context in mind. [para 61–63]
- The test for agreement and consensus ad idem is not subjective but **objective**: one party's words are construed in the sense the other would reasonably have understood. [para 56–58]

### 3. Sufficiency of consideration [para 64–86]
- [[law-concepts/part1-formation/consideration]] remains a standard requirement of a valid contract (a deed is exempt). The benefit-detriment analysis is the most practical definition. [para 64–67]
- **Sufficiency suffices and adequacy is immaterial** — legal sufficiency is distinct from a layperson's sense of the adequacy of consideration. [para 86]
- Application: the defendant signed the Waiver Letter **at the request of the promisor (the plaintiff)** → the defendant incurred a **legal detriment**, and even though the benefit nominally went to a third party (ASP), **the request creates the linkage between the parties**, making it sufficient consideration. The plaintiff also gained a benefit, namely his personal interest. [para 79–82]
- Related principles: a voluntary detriment/benefit not made at request is not consideration (*Combe v Combe*); past consideration is in principle not consideration but is valid if bound up in a single transaction (*Pao On*). [para 82–85]

### 4. Consideration *coda* — abolitionist arguments and alternatives [para 92–118]
- Although the fundamental difficulties of consideration did not arise *directly*, the court reviewed their **theoretical and practical difficulties** extensively by way of obiter. [para 92]
- If the **"factual (not legal) benefit"** concept of *Williams v Roffey* is combined with the proposition that "sufficiency suffices and adequacy is immaterial," consideration can be found virtually anywhere, risking rendering the **requirement otiose/redundant**. [para 100–101]
- Possible **alternatives**: [[law-concepts/part3-vitiating-factors/duress|economic duress]], [[law-concepts/part3-vitiating-factors/undue-influence|undue influence]], [[law-concepts/part3-vitiating-factors/unconscionability|unconscionability]], promissory estoppel, and a writing requirement. Each alternative, however, has its own difficulties. [para 111–116]
- Provisional conclusion: a somewhat diluted doctrine of consideration plus the alternative doctrines — i.e. the **status quo** — may be the most practical (though reform was not before the court for decision). [para 117–118]

## Exam Points / Significance

- The core ratio is that **compromise** is in essence a contract, and that whether one is formed is a matter of **objective construction looking at the whole correspondence**.
- On consideration, the analysis that **a "request" is the linkage joining detriment/benefit to the parties** is frequently used in answers (it does not matter that the benefit goes to a third party).
- The *coda* is the leading Singapore authority on consideration-abolition and alternatives — citable in the policy discussion of an IRAC answer.
- Related Singapore cases: [[cases/man-financial-v-wong]] (termination agreement = fresh contract), [[cases/teo-seng-kee-bob-v-arianecorp]] (applying *Williams*).
