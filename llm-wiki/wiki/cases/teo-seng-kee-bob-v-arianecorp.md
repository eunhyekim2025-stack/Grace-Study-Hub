# Teo Seng Kee Bob v Arianecorp Ltd [2008] SGHC 81

> **Court**: High Court (Lai Siu Chiu J) · **Decision**: 2008-05-30 · Suit 243/2007
> A Singapore decision dealing simultaneously with **practical/factual benefit** (applying *Williams v Roffey*) and **offer vs invitation to treat**. It shows how the old principle of *Stilk v Myrick* — that performance of an existing obligation cannot be consideration for a new promise — has evolved into modern consideration doctrine.

## One-line Holding

The issue was **when** the agreement for the plaintiff (Bob Teo) to buy 300,000 shares of abKey from the defendant Arianecorp for S$300,000 was formed (the defendant arguing an oral contract on 2006-10-12 vs the plaintiff arguing an agreement in November 2006). **Held: at the 2006-10-12 meeting no terms of sale were discussed at all, so the "agreement in principle" was no more than an invitation to treat, and the true contract was formed on 2006-12-20 (transfer of shares + delivery of inventory + release of the company's debt ↔ S$300,000).** The defendant's defence that there was no fresh consideration for the change to instalment payment was rejected under the *Williams v Roffey* exception, because the defendant obtained a **factual/practical benefit by using the plaintiff's S$250,000 in its cash flow**. The plaintiff succeeded and the counterclaim was dismissed. [para 79–95]

## Facts [para 1–45]

- The plaintiff was the MD and major shareholder of abKey (manufacturer of computer keyboards). The defendant Arianecorp (a listed company) had invested S$300,000 in abKey and held 300,000 shares. The defendant's nominee on the company's board was the COO, OTG (Ong Teck Guan). [para 1–5]
- A dispute arose between abKey and the defendant over delays and quality problems in keyboard manufacturing. The defendant withheld the company's inventory on the ground of unpaid sums, and the company disputed the debt and the inventory price. Deadlock. [para 9–11]
- 2006-10-12 board meeting at the Pan Pacific Hotel. The plaintiff offered to buy back the shares of the defendant and Ekong and handed **blank offer letters** to OTG and Ong. Ong signed Ekong's offer letter on the spot (150,000 shares, S$150,000). However, OTG, considering that the signature of the defendant's CEO (Kea) was needed, **did not sign the defendant's offer letter** and took the document away. [para 13–18]
- The minutes (drawn up by Cindy) recorded that "the director / Ariane agreed to sell the shares to Bob Teo at par value". The plaintiff did not dispute this and sent a thank-you email calling them "accurate minutes". [para 17]
- On 2006-10-25 OTG signed the defendant's offer letter, but the plaintiff refused to accept it (wishing to negotiate several issues). Further negotiations over release of debt and delivery of inventory followed (phone call on 11/1, emails on 11/2 and 11/10). [para 19–28]
- On 2006-12-19~20 the plaintiff paid a S$250,000 cashier's order and gave a personal undertaking to pay the balance of S$50,000 by 2007-03-31. The defendant drew up and delivered the share transfer forms and the director's resignation letter. [para 36–40]
- On 2007-01-25 OTG made a *volte face* ("new position"): asserting that the S$300,000 was consideration only for delivery of inventory and that the share transfer and debt release were separate. The plaintiff did not pay the balance and sued on 2007-04-17. [para 43–45]

## Court Below

> No conflict — a single-instance (High Court) case with no court below.

## Issues [para 78]

1. Was an **oral contract formed on 2006-10-12** (the defendant) or in **November 2006 (or on 12-20)** (the plaintiff)? — In essence, were the statements at the meeting an **offer or an invitation to treat**?
2. Was there **fresh consideration** for the change (such as instalment payment) — can performance of an existing obligation be valid consideration?

## Holding

- On 2006-10-12 **no contract was formed** — an "agreement in principle" with no terms of sale discussed is merely an invitation to treat. [para 79–81]
- Nor was one formed on 2006-11-02 or 11-10 (no unconditional acceptance). [para 82–87]
- **The contract was formed on 2006-12-20**: the defendant transfers shares + delivers inventory + releases the company's debt, the plaintiff pays S$300,000. [para 93]
- The fresh-consideration defence to the change was rejected — the defendant obtained a *factual/practical benefit*. [para 88–92]
- The plaintiff succeeded (order for delivery of share transfer forms, share certificates, and board resolution, delivery of inventory, and release of debt ↔ the plaintiff's payment of the S$50,000 balance). The **counterclaim was dismissed**, with costs to the plaintiff. [para 94–95]

## Ratio / Core Doctrine

### 1. Offer vs invitation to treat [para 79–81]
- An offer capable of being converted into an agreement by acceptance must be a **"definite promise to be bound if specified terms are accepted"** (citing *Cheshire, Fifoot & Furmston*). An "in principle" agreement in which the terms of sale (price, payment method, etc.) were not discussed at all amounts only to an **invitation to treat**. [para 81]
- Here: the terms of sale were not discussed at the meeting (the plaintiff considered that he could not finalise the terms before hearing back from OTG and Kea), and OTG did not sign the offer letter owing to the authority issue → there was no binding contract. The reason Ekong was treated differently is that Ekong had no outstanding issues (debt, inventory) with the company. [para 79–80] → [[law-concepts/ch07-offer-acceptance/offer]] · [[law-concepts/ch07-offer-acceptance/acceptance]]

### 2. Performance of an existing obligation and consideration — the old principle of *Stilk v Myrick* [para 88]
- The defendant's defence: there was no fresh consideration for the change (share transfer, inventory delivery, debt release) = the **old English rule** that "performance of an existing contractual obligation is not valid consideration for a new promise" (*Stilk v Myrick* (1809)). [para 88]

### 3. Practical / Factual Benefit — the *Williams v Roffey* exception [para 89–92]
- **Modern consideration doctrine has moved away from that old position.** Glidewell LJ's proposition in *Williams v Roffey Bros & Nicholls* [1990] (in substance): where A has contracted to supply work or goods to B, and before complete performance B promises additional payment and thereby B **in fact obtains a benefit or avoids a disbenefit**, and that promise was not obtained by economic duress or fraud, that benefit to B is consideration and the promise is binding. [para 89]
- The Singapore Court of Appeal in *Sea-Land Service Inc v Cheong Fook Chee Vincent* [1994] received *Williams* as a **limited exception** to the English rule (performance of an existing obligation ≠ consideration). Requirement to trigger the exception: **the promisor must obtain a *factual or practical benefit* from the promisee's performance of the existing obligation.** [para 90]
- Application here: OTG eventually admitted that the defendant **used the plaintiff's S$250,000 in its own cash flow from 2006-12-20** → the defendant clearly obtained a factual/practical benefit → the *Williams* exception is satisfied → the fresh-consideration defence is rejected. [para 91]
- Supplement: "modern contract law requires very little to find the existence of consideration" (citing V K Rajah JC in **[[cases/chwee-kin-keong-v-digilandmall|Chwee Kin Keong v Digilandmall]]** [2004] 2 SLR 594 at [139]). [para 92] → [[law-concepts/ch08-consideration-intention/consideration]]

### 4. Findings of fact — credibility [para 74–77]
- The plaintiff's testimony was largely credible (one undisclosed email about the minutes was improper but not fatal). OTG repeatedly shifted his position (goalpost-shifting) and was self-contradictory → low credibility. Ong was an objective and reliable witness. [para 74–77]

## Exam Points / Significance

- A Singapore application of **practical/factual benefit** — *Williams v Roffey* → *Sea-Land* (limited exception) → this case (use of cash flow = benefit). In an answer, apply the three elements of "performance of an existing obligation + real benefit to the promisor + absence of economic duress".
- **Offer vs invitation to treat**: an "in principle" agreement with terms not discussed is an invitation to treat — frequently appears in problems disputing the timing of a binding contract.
- Connected to [[law-concepts/ch08-consideration-intention/consideration]]'s "consideration must be sufficient but need not be adequate" and the modern trend of relaxing consideration. Singapore authority in the same vein: [[cases/gay-choon-ing-v-loh]] (consideration coda · request = the connecting link), [[cases/chwee-kin-keong-v-digilandmall]] (consideration is readily found).
- Related cases: *Williams v Roffey Bros* [1990], *Stilk v Myrick* (1809), *Sea-Land Service v Cheong Fook Chee Vincent* [1994], *Pao On v Lau Yiu Long* [1980].
