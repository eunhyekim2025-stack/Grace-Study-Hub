# Raiffeisen Zentralbank Osterreich AG v Archer Daniels Midland Co [2006] SGHC 182

> **Court**: High Court (Andrew Ang J) · **Decision**: 2006-11-01 · Judgment by: Andrew Ang J
> The Singapore leading case dissecting head-on the four elements of **fraudulent misrepresentation / deceit** and **conspiracy by unlawful means** (false representation, fraud, inducement, loss). It stresses in particular that **a misrepresentation alone does not make out a deceit claim**, and that fraud (dishonesty), actual inducement, and causal loss must all be proven.

## One-line holding

Even if the defendants inaccurately represented the structure of a structured trade-finance transaction (e.g. as to the passing of title), ① there was no **dishonesty** (merely an accident of timing), ② the plaintiff bank relied not on that structure but solely on **Parmalat's creditworthiness and the validity of the promissory notes and guarantees**, so there was **no actual inducement**, and ③ the loss arose not from the misrepresentation but from **Parmalat's insolvency**, so there is **no causation** → the deceit claim fails, and the dependent conspiracy claim fails with it. [para 51, 73, 86, 99]

## Facts [para 1–6]

- The plaintiff is the Austrian bank Raiffeisen Zentralbank ("RZB", Singapore branch). The defendants are the global agricultural-commodities trader **Archer Daniels Midland ("ADM")** and four of its affiliates (ADM-ACTI, Toepfer, Trade Resources LLC, Rush River). [para 1]
- Purpose of the transaction: a **structured trade finance** arrangement that "**borrowed**" ADM's trade flow to provide cheaper working capital to Parmalat's Brazilian subsidiary. The motive was to avoid high interest and high withholding tax. [para 2]
- Structure: Wishaw (a Parmalat affiliate) bought goods from ADM/affiliates on **360-day deferred terms** (issuing Parmalat-guaranteed promissory notes) → immediately resold the goods on sight terms to an ADM affiliate (RRT) to raise cash → RZB bought the promissory notes at a **without-recourse discount**. [para 4]
- Of the four transactions, the notes on the 1st and 2nd were repaid in full at maturity. But the **3rd and 4th** went unpaid following **Parmalat's insolvency** in 2003-12. [para 5]
- RZB brought two claims: ① damages for fraudulent misrepresentation and ② conspiracy by unlawful means. [para 6]

## Issues [para 7]

On fraudulent misrepresentation (deceit):
(a) what representation was made and was it false, (b) if false, was it made **fraudulently**, (c) was there **inducement** (intent + actual reliance), (d) did the plaintiff suffer **loss as a result** of the misrepresentation? Plus (e) whether conspiracy by unlawful means is made out.

## Holding

**Judgment for all defendants**, the plaintiff's claim dismissed (with costs). Neither misrepresentation nor conspiracy is made out. [para 100]

## Ratio (core doctrine)

### 1. Existence of a false representation — the implied "passing of title" representation becomes false later [para 8–37]
- The plaintiff's pleading was that the transaction was represented as a **genuine buy-sell** — the core being distilled as an **implied representation that title passes** between the third defendant and Wishaw. [para 23]
- The court held that such an implied representation could be read into the pleading. [para 21] And by the time the notes were discounted the goods had already been delivered to and paid for by the ultimate buyer, so there was in fact **no title to pass to Wishaw, making the representation false**. [para 32–33]
- **The continuing-representation doctrine** (Chitty, Clerk & Lindsell): even a representation true when first made becomes a misrepresentation if the representor knows it has become false before the contract is concluded and fails to disclose this — an innocent representation can convert into a fraudulent one. [para 36–37]

### 2. Fraud — dishonesty is the touchstone, negligence is not fraud [para 38–51]
- *Derry v Peek* (HL) / *Panatron v Lee Cheow Lee*: deceit is not made out by a mere false representation; it must be proven that the representor made it ① **knowingly**, ② **without belief in its truth**, or ③ **recklessly** as to its truth. An **honest belief** in its truth means there is no fraud. [para 38]
- **Dishonesty is the touchstone distinguishing fraudulent misrepresentation from other forms.** The test is belief, not knowledge, and good faith suffices so long as it is honest, even if unreasonable. **Gross negligence is not fraud either** — the belief must be so outlandish as to permit an inference of the absence of honest belief. [para 40–43]
- Here: the defendants **honestly believed** that title and the passing of title were **irrelevant to the plaintiff** (witnesses Spycher, Longwell, Soh), with no evidence of fraudulent intent. The suggestion that they staged a "conspiracy" out of a transaction a dozen other banks were also doing, for a trifling profit of about US$40,000, makes no sense. **The misrepresentation was merely an accident of timing.** [para 44–51]

### 3. Inducement — there must be actual reliance [para 52–73]
- Two aspects: (i) the representor's **intent to induce** (presumed where materiality is proven, shifting the burden), and (ii) whether the recipient **actually relied and changed its position** (actual inducement). [para 53–54]
- The misrepresentation need not be the **sole** cause, but it must form a **real and substantial part** (*JEB Fasteners*, *Avon Insurance*). Distinguish a factor that merely *supported or encouraged* (observed/encouraged) the decision from one that *induced* it. [para 55–60]
- Here: the plaintiff's credit committee focused solely on **Parmalat's creditworthiness + the validity of the promissory notes and guarantees**, and **regarded the transaction structure (title, circularity, payment flow) as unimportant** (witnesses Tan and Chew, credit documents, cross-examination). Chew's change of position on the second day ("would have declined because of the structure") was an *ex post facto* reconstruction lacking credibility. → **No actual inducement.** [para 61–73]

### 4. Intent to induce — the implied representation was not deliberate concealment [para 74–77]
- At the time of the original representation in 2001-09 there was no knowledge or intent of falsity; it became false at a later stage. Liability hangs on the "omission to disclose that it had become false", but there is **no evidence that this omission was done with intent to induce** (it was not deliberate suppression of information). [para 74–77]

### 5. Loss — failing on both causation and measure [para 78–93]
- Deceit damages restore the claimant to **the position it would have been in had the representation not been made** (out-of-pocket), not the contractual "loss of bargain" (McGregor). [para 87]
- **Causation**: the loss arose not from the misrepresentation but from **Parmalat's insolvency** — there is **no natural and proximate connection** between the misrepresentation and the loss (*Twycross v Grant*, *Waddell v Blockey*). The notes were transferable, immediately realisable assets and the plaintiff was free to sell. [para 82–86]
- **Measure**: the general principle in deceit is the **Date of Transaction Rule** — the difference between the contract price and the true market value (*Smith New Court v Citibank*). Here the market was not a false market and the plaintiff was not locked into the assets, so no exception is needed. The plaintiff bought the notes at a **discount (below market value)**, so there is no loss on the date-of-transaction basis — **loss not proven.** [para 89–93]
- An interesting aside: **even if the representation had been true**, the same loss would have occurred through Parmalat's insolvency → even a contractual suit would have recovered nothing. [para 88]

### 6. Conspiracy by unlawful means — dependent on deceit [para 94–99]
- Conspiracy (unlawful means) = two or more persons combining and carrying out an unlawful act with intent to injure the plaintiff and achieving that intent (*Quah Kay Tee*, *Kuwait Oil Tanker*). The agreement need not be express and may be inferred from conduct. [para 94–97]
- But here the conspiracy's **unlawful means is precisely the fraudulent misrepresentation**. Since it has already been found that there was no fraud or dishonesty, an essential element of the conspiracy is missing → **if deceit fails, the conspiracy collapses with it**. [para 99]

## Exam points / significance

- **Deceit four-element checklist**: (1) false representation (2) **fraud = dishonesty** (*Derry v Peek*, gross negligence ≠ fraud) (3) **actual inducement** (real & substantial part, presumption → rebuttal) (4) **causal loss** (date-of-transaction basis, out-of-pocket). Missing any one defeats the claim.
- Here the claim **failed simultaneously on all of (2), (3), and (4)** — a model of applying each element independently in an answer.
- **The continuing-representation doctrine**: even an initially true representation carries a duty to disclose once it becomes false — an innocent representation can convert into a fraudulent one (though here it foundered on the absence of *intent to knowingly conceal*).
- **The key inducement distinction**: a factor that *supported or encouraged* a decision ≠ a factor that actually *induced* it. The factual analysis of identifying the "object of actual reliance" through credit-committee memos and cross-examination is decisive.
- **Causation and measure of loss**: where a separate cause (Parmalat's insolvency) intervenes, proximity is broken. Date of Transaction Rule + the false-market exception.
- A conspiracy that **takes fraud as its unlawful means is dependent on deceit** — if deceit fails, the conspiracy fails automatically.
- Adjacent case: [[cases/orient-centre-v-societe-generale]] — the same *Peekay*/bank-misrepresentation context, but there the claim was barred (absence of reliance) by a **non-reliance clause and striking out**, whereas here it failed on the merits for **failure to prove fraud, inducement, and loss**.
