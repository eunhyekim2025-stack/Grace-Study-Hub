# RDC Concrete Pte Ltd v Sato Kogyo (S) Pte Ltd [2007] SGCA 39

> **Court**: Court of Appeal (Chan Sek Keong CJ, Andrew Phang Boon Leong JA, V K Rajah JA) · **Decision**: 2007-08-29 · Judgment by: Phang JA
> Singapore's leading case on **discharge by breach** — it establishes the **four Situations** framework under which an innocent party may terminate a contract, and integrates and ranks the **condition/warranty** approach with the **Hongkong Fir (intermediate term)** approach. Incidentally, it also sets out the relationship between force majeure clauses and frustration.

## One-line holding

A breach of contract does not automatically terminate the contract; there are only four Situations in which the innocent party may **elect** to terminate. This case falls within **Situation 1** (an express termination clause), so the plaintiff (Sato Kogyo)'s termination was lawful, and the defendant (RDC) could not invoke the force majeure clause or frustration. [para 90, 111, 116]

## Facts [para 1–13]

- **Sato Kogyo (plaintiff)**, the main contractor for the Lorong Chuan station of the MRT Circle Line (the Project), contracted with the ready-mixed concrete supplier **RDC (defendant)** (cl 2: 2003-09-01 to 2006-06-30, supply of about 70,000m³, fixed price). [para 1–3]
- In May–June 2004 many cube strength tests failed → the LTA suspended the supply of structural concrete from 2004-07-07 to 11-17 (Pan United was the only approved supplier). The plaintiff sourced from the more expensive Pan United and charged the cost differential to the defendant. [para 4–6]
- Even after supply resumed (2004-11-18), the defendant failed to supply on **as many as 42 occasions** (raw-material shortages, plant breakdowns). The plaintiff deducted the cost differential of alternative sourcing from sums owed to the defendant (cl 8). [para 7–8]
- The defendant refused to bear the differential and stopped supplying on 2005-04-05. On 2005-05-30 the plaintiff **terminated the contract under cl 8**: (a) continuing failure to supply, (b) failure to meet the LTA's requirements. [para 9–11]

## Lower court (High Court, [2006] SGHC 213)

The plaintiff largely succeeded in part across five issues. In particular, on issue (e) (termination) the plaintiff's right to terminate was recognised. The Court of Appeal comprehensively restated the law on force majeure, the differential, and termination, finding further for the plaintiff on (b) and (c). [para 15–20, 139]

## Issues [para 14, 21]

(a) Is it an exclusive/sole-supply contract? (b) Is the suspension-period differential claimable? (c) Does the force majeure clause provide an excuse? (d) Was the defendant's stoppage of supply on 2005-04-05 justified? (e) Was the plaintiff's **termination** on 2005-05-30 justified?

## Holding

CA 151 (defendant's appeal) dismissed, CA 152 (plaintiff's appeal) allowed in part. (a) Not an exclusive contract (plaintiff loses). (b) Termination is not a precondition to the differential claim — the differential is fully claimable as a "direct cost" under cl 8 (plaintiff wins). (c) Force majeure cannot be invoked because it was **not pleaded**, and even hypothetically, raw-material shortages and plant breakdowns do not meet the "beyond control" requirement (plaintiff wins). (d) The plaintiff's unilateral, retrospective imposition of a 6% management fee was unjustified, so the defendant's stoppage of supply was lawful (defendant wins). (e) The plaintiff's **termination was lawful** (defendant loses). [para 139]

## Ratio (core doctrine)

### 1. Discharge by breach — the Four Situations [para 90–113]

Even where there is a breach, there is **no automatic termination**. The cases in which the innocent party may elect to terminate fall into two categories, totalling **four Situations**. [para 90]

| Situation | When termination is justified | Focus |
|------|----------------------|------|
| **Situation 1** | A contract term **expressly and clearly** provides that "on the occurrence of a specified event the innocent party may terminate" (an express termination clause) | The wording of the clause — operates **independently** of the other Situations |
| **Situation 2** | The party in breach by word or conduct **clearly evinces an intention not to perform the contract at all (renunciation)** | The intention to refuse performance |
| **Situation 3(a)** | The breached term is a **condition** (the condition-warranty approach) | **The nature of the term** (regardless of consequence; ex ante, hypothetical) |
| **Situation 3(b)** | The **consequence** of the breach deprives the innocent party of "substantially the whole benefit it was intended to obtain under the contract" (the *Hongkong Fir* approach) | **The nature and consequence of the breach** (ex post, wait-and-see) |

- Situation 1 is exemplified here by cl 8 — the gravity of the consequences is not examined, and Situation 3(b) does not apply. [para 91, 116–118]
- Situation 2 (renunciation) does not apply here — the defendant said it would resume supply if only it received the sums owed, so it did not evince an intention "not to perform at all". [para 119]

### 2. Condition / Warranty / Intermediate (Innominate) Term [para 97–105]

- **Condition**: a term so important that, on the parties' intention, **any breach whatever, regardless of consequence**, gives rise to a right to terminate. [para 97]
- **Warranty**: a term that, on the parties' intention, **never** gives rise to a right to terminate **however grave** the breach. This condition-warranty approach is codified in the Sale of Goods Act. [para 98]
- **Intermediate / Innominate Term**: neither a condition nor a warranty, its character being determined by the **actual consequences** of the breach (*Hongkong Fir*). [para 104–105]
- The difference between the two approaches: condition-warranty looks at the intention **at the time of contracting** (ex ante, predictive, certainty), while *Hongkong Fir* looks at the **consequences after the breach** (ex post, fairness). [para 100]

### 3. Integration and ranking of the two approaches — Situation 3(a) first, 3(b) later [para 106–112]

- **The paramount consideration is giving effect to the parties' intention** → the condition-warranty approach (Situation 3(a)) applies **first**. If the term is a condition, that establishes the right to terminate, and that is the end of it. [para 106, 112]
- If the term is **not a condition** (i.e. it is a warranty), then for fairness the court **additionally** examines the consequences of the breach under the *Hongkong Fir* approach (Situation 3(b)). If the consequences deprive the party of substantially the whole benefit, termination is possible even for a warranty; if trivial, no termination. [para 107]
- As a result the traditional concept of "warranty" effectively disappears, and a term that is not a condition is effectively treated as an **intermediate term**. The right to damages, however, always remains. [para 108]

### 4. Damages on breach are a right as of course [para 40, 114]

- The innocent party can always claim damages for breach **as of right**, **whether or not it is entitled to terminate, and even if entitled, whether or not it does** (*Raineri v Miles*). The cl 8 differential claim is a separate right not premised on termination (the core of issue (b) here). [para 40, 41, 114]

### 5. Distinguishing force majeure clauses from frustration [para 53–66] → [[law-concepts/ch17-frustration/frustration]]

- A force majeure clause is a contractual allocation of risk for **foreseeable** events, whereas frustration is the common-law doctrine for **unforeseeable** external events. [para 56]
- But the principles of the doctrine of frustration (beyond control, not self-induced) generally apply **to the construction** of a force majeure clause too. For a force majeure clause to exclude frustration, **clear and unequivocal wording** is required. [para 57, 63]
- The key difference is the **nature of the remedy**: frustration **automatically terminates** the contract by operation of law and the Frustrated Contracts Act (Cap 115) applies, whereas if a force majeure clause applies the obligations are merely "suspended/restricted" and the contract survives. [para 60–61]
- A party invoking force majeure must prove (i) that it falls within the scope of the clause and (ii) that it **took all reasonable steps to avoid or mitigate the consequences** (*Channel Island Ferries*). Here the raw-material shortage was merely "more onerous", not beyond control, and the plant breakdowns were avoidable, so there is no excuse. [para 64, 70, 79–80]

### 6. Force majeure must be specifically pleaded [para 50–52]

- Force majeure (and frustration) must be **specifically pleaded** to be invoked (O 18 r 8(1) Rules of Court). Here the defendant did not plead it, and loses on this ground alone. [para 50–51]

## Exam points / significance

- **The four-Situations table is the skeleton of an answer** — in a discharge-by-breach question, examine in the order Situation 1 (express termination clause) → Situation 2 (renunciation) → Situation 3(a) (condition) → Situation 3(b) (*Hongkong Fir*). Applying 3(a) **first** is the exam point.
- The condition/warranty/innominate-term distinction connects directly to [[law-concepts/ch10-terms/terms]]. A condition is "important regardless of consequence", an innominate term "looks at the consequences".
- Force majeure ≠ frustration: the difference in the nature of the remedy (suspension vs automatic termination), the specific-pleading requirement, and proof of the duty to avoid/mitigate are the key points.
- The later case [[cases/sports-connection-v-deuter]] refines this four-Situation framework (especially the fate of warranty and the intermediate term), and [[cases/man-financial-v-wong]] adds factors for identifying a condition.
