# BOM v BOK [2018] SGCA 83

> **Court**: Court of Appeal — Civil Appeals Nos 3 & 5 of 2018 (Andrew Phang Boon Leong JA, Steven Chong JA, Belinda Ang Saw Ean J, Chan Seng Onn J, Quentin Loh J) · **Argued**: 2018-09-10 · **Judgment**: 2018-11-29 · Judgment authored by: Phang JA
> The leading Singapore case on **unconscionability**. The Court of Appeal **rejected the broad doctrine** of the Australian *Amadio* line and instead **adopted the narrow doctrine** of *Fry v Lane* and *Cresswell v Potter*, refining it by **extending "infirmity" beyond poverty and ignorance to encompass physical, mental, and emotional vulnerability**. On the same facts (setting aside a declaration of trust), all four grounds — misrepresentation, mistake, **Class 1 undue influence**, and unconscionability — were made out. [para 142, 154–156]

## One-Line Summary

One week after his mother's death, a wealthy 29-year-old husband in a state of acute grief was deceived by his wife's **misrepresentation** that "this trust only takes effect on death" into executing a declaration of trust (DOT) transferring his entire estate to their young son. The Court of Appeal upheld the trial court's setting aside of the DOT on all of **misrepresentation, mistake, Class 1 undue influence, and unconscionability**, and **dismissed the appeal** (costs of S$60,000 borne by the husband). It did, however, reverse the trial court's finding of **Class 2A undue influence (implied retainer)**. [para 91–92, 107, 113, 156–157]

## Facts [para 1–20]

- **Husband (respondent/plaintiff)**: a wealthy 29-year-old. His mother's funeral was on 2014-03-23; her estate was worth about S$54m. [para 1]
- On the evening of 2014-03-26, shortly after learning the contents of his mother's will, he executed a **Declaration of Trust ("DOT")** — in substance **immediately transferring his entire estate to their young son (the Son)**. [para facts, 27]
- Husband's case: his **Wife (BOM)** represented that "the DOT was merely a safeguard that would only take effect on death, and that until then he could freely use the assets" ("the Misrepresentation"), thereby deceiving him. She pressured him by threatening to evict him from the home (Stevens Road Property) if he refused to sign. [para 24, 74]
- The marriage later broke down. On 2015-11-30 the husband brought suit to set aside the DOT (misrepresentation, mistake, undue influence, unconscionability). [para 20]

## Procedural History / Court Below (High Court, *BOK v BOL* [2017] SGHC 316) [para 2, 27]

- The trial judge (the Judge) found for the husband, **setting aside the DOT on all four grounds: misrepresentation, mistake, undue influence (Class 1 & 2A), and unconscionability**. [para 27]
- On unconscionability the trial court established a three-stage test: (1) one party's vulnerability (including acute grief); (2) exploitation of that vulnerability; (3) thereafter, the defendant bears the burden of proving the transaction was **fair, just, and reasonable**. [para 27(d)]
- The Wife (BOM) and the Son (BOL) appealed against the entire judgment. The applications to adduce fresh evidence and the objection that fraud had not been pleaded were both **dismissed**. [para 2, 35–45]

## Issues [para 2, 46]

On which ground may the declaration of trust be set aside — (a) misrepresentation, (b) mistake, (c) undue influence (Class 1 / Class 2A), or (d) unconscionability? Rather than the law, the appellants chiefly contested the **findings of fact** (the husband's understanding of the trust and the circumstances on the day of signing). [para 46]

## Holding

**Appeal dismissed.** The DOT is set aside on each of the following grounds, **each independently**: (a) **Misrepresentation** [91]; (b) **Mistake** [92]; (c) **Class 1 undue influence** [107]; (d) **Unconscionability** [156]. However, (e) **Class 2A undue influence is not made out** — there is no implied retainer between spouses [113]. Costs of S$60,000 borne by the husband. [para 157]

## Law (Ratio / Core Doctrine)

### 1. Misrepresentation [para 89–91] → [[law-concepts/misrepresentation]]

- Elements of fraudulent misrepresentation (not disputed): (a) a representation of fact by word or conduct, (b) intent that the other party act on it, (c) the plaintiff actually acting on it, (d) damage, and (e) making the representation **knowing it to be false**. [para 89]
- The wife caused the husband to rely on the representation that "the DOT only takes effect on death" **knowing it to be false** — the recorded conversation of 2015-02-12 ("if you don't sign, leave the house") was decisive. All elements satisfied → the DOT may be set aside on this ground alone. [para 74–75, 91]

### 2. Mistake (mistake in a voluntary disposition) [para 90–92] → [[law-concepts/mistake]]

- Adopting *Pitt v Holt* [2013] (via *BMM v BMN*): equity may set aside a voluntary disposition where there is (1) a **causative mistake** (as to the legal character of the transaction, or as to a fact or matter of law basic to the transaction) that is (2) **so serious that it would be unconscionable to refuse relief**. [para 90]
- The husband's mistake ("the DOT only takes effect on death") was causative, lay at the heart of a transaction that immediately stripped him of his entire estate, and had grave consequences → set aside. [para 92]

### 3. Undue Influence [para 94–113] → [[law-concepts/undue-influence]]

**Two categories** [para 101]:
- **Class 1 (actual)**: the plaintiff proves (i) capacity to influence, (ii) exercise of influence, (iii) that the exercise was undue, and (iv) that it brought about the transaction. [para 101(a)]
- **Class 2 (presumed)**: (i) a relationship of trust and confidence, (ii) of a kind in which abuse is presumed, (iii) a transaction calling for explanation. **2A** = relationships in which the law **irrebuttably presumes** a relationship of trust (solicitor–client, etc.; **spouses are excluded**); **2B** = the plaintiff proves the relationship of trust. [para 101(b)]

**Key principle — the influencer need not benefit** [para 102–103]:
- *Bridgeman v Green* (1757): *"whoever receives it, must take it tainted and infected with the undue influence"* — even where the recipient of a gift did not exercise the influence, a gift procured by undue influence is void. The mischief of undue influence lies in the **"abuse of a position of trust"**, not in who benefits. [para 102–103]

**Class 1 — made out** [para 104–107]:
- The key question was whether the husband was **vulnerable due to acute grief**. It did not matter that he harboured doubts or lacked mental capacity — **undue influence does not arise only from mental incapacity**, and *"bullying or importunity may impair a person's free will"*. Knowing the husband was lonely and dependent on her and their son as his only remaining family, the wife coerced his signature with the threat of eviction → she **exploited his loneliness** amid his grief. [para 106–107]

**Class 2A — not made out** [para 108–113]:
- The trial court held there was an **implied retainer** between spouses giving rise to a presumed relationship of trust, but the Court of Appeal reversed. Under the **domestic-context** doctrine of *Balfour v Balfour*, it would be wrong to deem a solicitor–client relationship merely because a legally knowledgeable spouse gave advice. → no implied retainer, Class 2A not made out (though the possibility is left open in extremely rare cases). [para 109–113]

### 4. Unconscionability — adoption and refinement of the narrow doctrine [para 114–156] → [[law-concepts/unconscionability]]

**(a) The two senses of "unconscionability"** [para 117–122]:
- **Unconscionability as a rationale** — the spirit of justice and fairness underlying existing doctrines such as undue influence and duress ("one must not unfairly exploit the weak party's plight"). This *cannot itself be a doctrine*. [para 118–119]
- **Unconscionability as an independent doctrine** — it operates clearly only on plainly egregious facts, and because it **fails to provide a sufficient legal standard** it breeds uncertainty and unpredictability. Most cases are not "plainly egregious," and the doctrine breaks down at that point. [para 120–122]

**(b) Narrow doctrine vs broad doctrine** [para 127–139]:
- **Narrow doctrine**: *Fry v Lane* (1888) — where a "poor and ignorant" person disposes of property at a considerable undervalue without independent advice, equity sets it aside, shifting the burden to the defendant to prove the transaction was **"fair, just, and reasonable"**. *Cresswell v Potter* (1978) restated this as three requirements ((1) poor and ignorant, (2) considerable undervalue, (3) absence of independent advice). [para 129–131]
- **Broad doctrine**: the Australian *Amadio* (1983) — "special disability" plus the stronger party's awareness shifts the burden. The Court of Appeal **rejected this as too subjective and broad** (risking "inequality of bargaining power," approaching *Lloyds Bank v Bundy*). The Singapore High Court has also consistently rejected the broad doctrine. [para 132–134, 148]

**(c) The adopted formulation — the refined narrow doctrine** [para 140–144]:
- **Expand the first requirement (the "poor and ignorant" of Fry/Cresswell)**: beyond poverty and ignorance, include **physical, mental, and emotional vulnerability (infirmity)**. But not *every* vulnerability — it must be **serious enough to sharply impair the plaintiff's "ability to conserve their own interests"**, and must have been or ought to have been **evident to the other party** (*Blomley v Ryan*). [para 141]
- **The second and third requirements (considerable undervalue, absence of independent advice) are not essential** — though they are very important factors the court will **"invariably consider"**. [para 141–142]
- **Test summary** [para 142]: if the plaintiff proves (1) **an infirmity that the other party exploited in procuring the transaction**, then (2) the burden shifts to the defendant to prove the transaction was **fair, just and reasonable**. (Undervalue and absence of advice are not essential requirements but are always considered in assessing whether the transaction was improvident.)
- **"Middle ground"**: it is subtly but decisively distinguished from the broad doctrine in that the narrow doctrine is applied through the lens of *Fry* and *Cresswell* and not through the lens of *Amadio* and *Alec Lobb* (the broad doctrine) — a middle course grounded not in theory but in **practical application**. [para 143–144]

**(d) Historical hypothesis — is the narrow doctrine = Class 1 undue influence?** [para 145–152]:
- The narrow doctrine may in fact have been **not a separate doctrine but a species of Class 1 undue influence** (catching cases of improvident bargains and expectant heirs). If so, it is a "redundant" doctrine. [para 145–147]
- Nonetheless, the narrow doctrine **produces no clear legal contradiction and is accepted throughout the Commonwealth**, so it need not be discarded. Though **virtually coincident** with Class 1 undue influence, the point is left only as a hypothesis. **Singapore law is currently the narrow doctrine as refined at [141]–[144].** [para 149, 152]

**(e) Application to the present case** [para 154–156]:
- The husband's **acute grief** was a **serious infirmity** sufficient to impair his decision-making capacity, and the wife knew this and **used his sense of isolation as leverage**. [para 154]
- The husband had **no independent advice**, and the DOT was a **considerable-undervalue (gratuitous transfer of the entire estate)** transaction — neither essential, but both strongly supporting exploitation. The DOT was **not fair, just and reasonable**. → setting aside on unconscionability upheld. [para 155–156]

### 5. Coda — rejection of an umbrella doctrine [coda following para 157]

- Is an overarching doctrine binding together duress, undue influence, and unconscionability desirable? While the connections among the three doctrines are acknowledged, such an umbrella doctrine **lacks any workable standard and would breed excessive subjectivity and uncertainty**, and is therefore **rejected**. [coda, para 177]

## Exam Points / Significance

- **Skeleton of an unconscionability answer**: (1) the plaintiff proves an **infirmity** — poverty/ignorance plus physical/mental/emotional, serious enough to **sharply impair the ability to conserve one's own interests**, and evident to the other party — and the other party **exploited** it in procuring the transaction → (2) the burden shifts, and the defendant must prove the transaction was **fair, just and reasonable**. **Undervalue and absence of advice are not essential (but are "very important factors").**
- The decision's core contribution: **expanding infirmity** (poverty/ignorance → physical/mental/emotional) + **explicitly rejecting the broad doctrine (*Amadio*)** + reformulating the narrow doctrine as a **middle ground**.
- **Unconscionability ≈ Class 1 undue influence**: the same facts almost always satisfy both doctrines (the present case being an example). The relationship between them remains a hypothesis. → in an answer, it is safe to discuss both doctrines **in parallel**.
- **Distinct** from unconscionability in the performance-bond context (*JBE Properties*, *BS Mount Sophia*) — this decision expressly excludes that context. [para 128]
- **An implied retainer between spouses** is not recognised in principle (*Balfour v Balfour* domestic context) → Class 2A is hard to make out between spouses. [para 111–113]
- Comparison: read alongside [[cases/gay-choon-ing-v-loh]], which deals broadly with vitiating factors, to complete the full map of vitiating factors.
