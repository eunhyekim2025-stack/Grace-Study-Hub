# Man Mohan Singh v Zurich Insurance (Singapore) Pte Ltd [2008] SGCA 24

> **Court**: Court of Appeal (Chan Sek Keong CJ, Andrew Phang Boon Leong JA, V K Rajah JA) · **Decision**: 2008-05-30 · Judgment by: Phang JA
> A sister judgment handed down right after [[cases/spandeck-v-dsta|Spandeck]] and [[cases/ngiam-kong-seng-v-lim|Ngiam]]. In the context of negligence damages, it applies and illustrates the [[cases/spandeck-v-dsta|Spandeck]] test and the **remoteness** control device through (i) the multiplier/multiplicand in assessing **loss of dependency**, (ii) **psychiatric harm** (grief vs a recognisable psychiatric illness), and (iii) a novel claim for **fertility-treatment costs**.

## One-line holding

- **Loss of dependency**: the multiplier/multiplicand may be adjusted *upward* on the facts (no reason uniformly to apply conservative estimates favouring the defendant); it is preferable to use a separate multiplier for each dependant. → Apportionment raised to 35%, multiplier raised to 8 years for the father and 13 years for the mother. [para 8–30]
- **Psychiatric harm**: recovery requires a **"recognisable psychiatric illness"** — mere *grief* or sorrow will not do (risk of double recovery; overlap with the bereavement claim). Here the medical reports failed to prove a recognisable psychiatric illness, and the first-stage [[cases/spandeck-v-dsta|Spandeck]] proximity was also lacking → dismissed. [para 31–42]
- **Fertility-treatment costs (novel claim)**: there is no legal right to "replace" a child — the claim fails on **all** stages of [[cases/spandeck-v-dsta|Spandeck]] (foreseeability, proximity, policy) and on **remoteness**. [para 43–59]

## Facts [para 1, 3–4]

- On the evening of 2002-12-02, a car driven by the second respondent (a careless driver) lost control and hit a tree → the appellant couple's **two only sons (Gurjiv, 17, and Pardip, 14), rear-seat passengers, died**. The couple lost both their children. [para 1, 3]
- The second respondent left the country and did not contest the action (interlocutory judgment in default). The vehicle's insurer, the **first respondent (Zurich/QBE)**, joined as a party to contest quantum. [para 4]
- The appellants claimed (a) bereavement, (b) funeral expenses, (c) loss of dependency, (d) **damages for post-traumatic shock and depression**, and (e) **fertility-treatment costs (about S$32,847.90)** incurred in trying to have another child. [para 1]

## Lower court (AR / High Court) [para 5–6]

- The AR (assistant registrar): bereavement S$20,000 (Civil Law Act s 21(4)), funeral expenses, loss of dependency (Gurjiv S$68,508 and Pardip S$78,165, apportionment 30%, multiplier 11/9 years). The claims for post-traumatic shock and depression and for transport costs were **dismissed**. The **fertility-treatment costs were allowed** (on the basis that a careless driver could foresee causing the loss of leaving parents childless). [para 5–6]
- The judge at first instance: upheld loss of dependency etc., but reversed the AR and dismissed the **fertility-treatment costs as too remote** (allowing the insurer's appeal). [para 6]

## Issues [para 7]

1. Whether the assessment of **loss of dependency** (apportionment, multiplier, multiplicand) was correct. [para 8–30]
2. Whether **post-traumatic shock and depression** is recoverable psychiatric harm. [para 31–42]
3. Whether **fertility-treatment costs** are recoverable (duty of care, remoteness). [para 43–59]

## Holding

The appeal on loss of dependency (CA 85/2007) is **allowed in part** (apportionment raised to 35%, multiplier raised to 8 years for the father and 13 years for the mother; the insurer's cross-appeal CA 86/2007 is dismissed). The **claims for post-traumatic shock and depression and for fertility-treatment costs are dismissed**. [para 60–61]

## Ratio (core doctrine)

### 1. Assessment of Loss of Dependency [para 8–30]
- **Multiplicand**: a deceased minor's future earnings are reasonably estimated from their *last verified academic results* (using MOM wage reports). There is no reason uniformly to favour the defendant with conservative estimates — no basis to treat the deceased differently from the ITE student in *Ho Yeow Kim*. [para 12–17]
- **Apportionment**: the AR's 30% is raised to 40% in line with *Ho Yeow Kim* and *Tan Harry*, but set at **35%** to account for the likelihood that the children would have married and become independent in their mid-twenties. [para 15–18]
- **Multiplier**: it is preferable to set a *separate* multiplier for each dependant (reflecting dependant-specific factors such as age and life expectancy). The usual range is 8–12 years. **Life expectancy is considered as at the date of the accident** but is just one of several variables. A longer life expectancy justifies a somewhat higher multiplier. → father (age 46) **8 years**, mother (age 44, not employed since 1988 and heavily financially dependent) **13 years**. [para 19–28]
- This assessment, however, is specific to *uncommon facts* and is **not a benchmark for the future**. [para 30]

### 2. Psychiatric harm — "grief" is not recoverable [para 31–42]
- Applying the [[cases/ngiam-kong-seng-v-lim|Ngiam]] and [[cases/spandeck-v-dsta|Spandeck]] framework. Recovery requires a **recognisable psychiatric illness**, and **grief, sorrow, and deprivation** — the *ordinary, inevitable incidents of life* — are **not recoverable** (Lord Oliver, *Alcock*). [para 32, 37]
- **Risk of double recovery**: the Civil Law Act s 21 already provides **bereavement** compensation (S$10,000), so to award grief again in negligence would duplicate it. [para 38]
- Here the medical reports mention the appellants' "depression and mourning" but fail to prove a **recognisable psychiatric illness**. In addition, of the three *McLoughlin* elements there is **no physical or causal proximity** (they were not at the scene and did not witness the immediate aftermath) → first-stage [[cases/spandeck-v-dsta|Spandeck]] proximity not satisfied → no duty of care. [para 36, 41]
- The use of independent psychiatric expert evidence is recommended (reaffirming *Ngiam* [97]). [para 42]

### 3. Fertility-treatment costs — dismissal of the novel claim [para 43–59]
- Distinguish **two levels of foreseeability**: (i) foreseeing that the defendant would harm the *tort victim* = a **duty of care** question; (ii) foreseeing the *kind of damage* = a **remoteness** question. [para 45]
- **Duty of care — no factual foreseeability / no proximity** [para 48]: even if a careless driver could foresee that a victim might have *surviving parents*, requiring foreseeability that those were the parents' *only children* and that the parents would *depend on assisted reproduction* goes **beyond the limits of foreseeability**. It cannot be said that the driver assumed a broad responsibility "not to render a couple childless" or that the parents relied on him → **no legal proximity**. [para 48]
- **No public policy (second stage)** [para 49–52]: the AR sought to limit claimants to "bereaved relatives of a particular state of mind", but such a distinction yields arbitrary and unfair results (parents with other children, a spouse who intends to remarry, etc.). There is **no legal right to "replace" a child** — human beings are irreplaceable. Plus it would be untenable to hold the defendant liable for the *plaintiff's subjective post-event choice* (IVF success rates were extremely low). [para 49–52]
- **Remoteness** [para 53–55]: under *The Wagon Mound* (reasonable foreseeability), *that the couple would become childless* is itself unforeseeable, so the fertility-treatment costs are also **too remote**. It is clarified, however, that "not consequential" is not the same as "too remote". [para 54–55]
- Distinguished from *Tan Hun Hoe v Harte* (negligent surgery causing infertility → assisted-reproduction costs allowed): that case involved a *professional relationship* where the doctor knew of the couple's wish to have children and the harm was foreseeable — an entirely different position from a careless driver. [para 58–59]

## Exam points / significance

- An authority that sets out the **mechanics of assessing loss of dependency** (multiplicand × multiplier × apportionment) and practical principles such as a *separate multiplier per dependant* and *life expectancy as at the date of accident*. Cited in [[law-concepts/ch18-remedies/remedies]] damages-assessment questions.
- The distinction between **grief vs a recognisable psychiatric illness**, and the argument for **avoiding double recovery with the bereavement claim**, are the key points.
- A model of **the methodology of filtering a novel claim through the single [[cases/spandeck-v-dsta|Spandeck]] test** — applying foreseeability, proximity, policy, and remoteness in turn. The policy proposition that a child cannot be "replaced" is frequently cited in answers.
- It clarifies that **the foreseeability of duty of care ≠ the foreseeability of remoteness** (a two-level distinction) — useful for separating issues in IRAC.
