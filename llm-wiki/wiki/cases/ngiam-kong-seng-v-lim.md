# Ngiam Kong Seng v Lim Chiew Hock [2008] SGCA 23

> **Court**: Court of Appeal (Chan Sek Keong CJ, Andrew Phang Boon Leong JA, V K Rajah JA) · **Decision**: 2008-05-29 · Judgment by: Phang JA
> The leading case applying the single two-stage [[cases/spandeck-v-dsta|Spandeck]] test to the field of **psychiatric harm / nervous shock**. It absorbs the three elements of *McLoughlin v O'Brian* into first-stage **proximity** and **rejects the primary/secondary victim distinction** of *Page v Smith*.

## One-line holding

The duty of care for psychiatric harm is also determined **not by a separate body of law but by the single two-stage [[cases/spandeck-v-dsta|Spandeck]] test** — ① a recognisable psychiatric illness + factual foreseeability (preliminary threshold), ② first-stage **legal proximity** (into which *McLoughlin*'s three elements — relational, physical, and causal proximity — are integrated), ③ second-stage **public policy**. Psychiatric harm caused by a third party's **mere communication of information** is denied a duty on foreseeability, proximity, and policy (absent malign intent) → the second appellant's claim is dismissed. [para 96–145]

## Facts [para 4–9]

- The first appellant (Ngiam, age 61) became **tetraplegic** in a traffic accident while riding his motorcycle. The second appellant (Quek, his wife) became his main carer. The respondent (Lim) was the taxi driver. [para 4–6]
- Immediately after the accident the respondent posed as a **good Samaritan**, acting as though he had merely helped → the second appellant felt grateful to him. When subsequent investigation by lawyers revealed that the respondent had been the **party to the accident**, the second appellant claimed to feel "betrayed" and to suffer clinical depression involving **severe depression and suicidal tendencies**. [para 7]
- The second appellant claimed **damages for clinical depression** on the grounds that (a) the respondent failed to disclose his involvement in the accident and the severity of the first appellant's injuries, and (b) he led her to believe he was a good Samaritan. [para 8]

## Lower court (High Court, [2007] SGHC 38) [para 11–13]

- Only liability was in issue. Finding the first appellant's account inconsistent, and that independent witnesses (the passenger Ms Andrew, the traffic police investigator) and physical evidence (the absence of vehicle damage) supported the respondent's version (that the motorcycle self-skidded), the judge **dismissed the first appellant's claim**. [para 12]
- The second appellant's claim, being dependent on the first appellant's, **failed automatically**; and even if liability were established, it would be dismissed because (a) it had no legal basis and was too remote, (b) the depression arose from her inability to cope with the burden of caregiving, and (c) it was an afterthought. [para 13]

## Issues [para 14, 19, 22]

1. Can the trial court's **finding of fact** (that the accident was not the respondent's fault) be overturned (the plainly-wrong standard)? [para 14]
2. Does the two-stage [[cases/spandeck-v-dsta|Spandeck]] test apply to the duty of care for **psychiatric harm**, and does the respondent here owe the second appellant a duty of care? [para 22, 130]

## Holding

Appeal **dismissed**. (1) The trial court's finding of fact was not plainly wrong → the first appellant fails. (2) The **single two-stage Spandeck test applies equally** to psychiatric harm, and here factual foreseeability, legal proximity, and public policy are all unsatisfied, so the respondent owes the second appellant no duty of care. [para 142, 148]

## Ratio (core doctrine)

### 1. Recognisable psychiatric illness — preliminary threshold [para 31, 97]
- As a precondition for recovery the plaintiff must prove a **"recognisable psychiatric illness"** (mere grief or sorrow excluded). In practice it is appropriate to treat this as a *threshold requirement* taken first, before *damage*, and the use of **independent psychiatric expert** evidence is preferable. [para 97]

### 2. Application of the Spandeck test to the field of psychiatric harm [para 20–21, 109–110, 121]
- Analysing Lord Wilberforce's formulation in *McLoughlin v O'Brian* / *Anns*, the court holds that the first stage he described means **proximity**, not *factual foreseeability alone*, and that "policy" conflates **two concepts** (① *legal policy* between the parties = proximity, ② *public policy* of society at large). [para 36–57]
- Conclusion: the duty of care for psychiatric harm is also determined by the **two-stage Spandeck test** — no separate body of law is needed. ① first-stage proximity, ② second-stage public policy. The whole process is incremental. [para 109, 121]

### 3. Absorbing McLoughlin's three elements into first-stage proximity [para 46–48, 99–103]
- The **three elements** Lord Wilberforce set out in *McLoughlin* Part 3 are in fact elements of **proximity**, not *public policy*, and correspond to Deane J's three-fold classification (*Sutherland*):
  - (a) the **class of persons** whose claims will be recognised = **relational/circumstantial proximity** [para 101]
  - (b) that person's **proximity to the accident** in time and space = **physical proximity** [para 102]
  - (c) the **means by which shock is caused** = **causal proximity** [para 103]

### 4. Rejection of the primary/secondary victim distinction in Page v Smith [para 59–95, 110]
- *Page v Smith* allows a primary victim to recover for psychiatric harm on the foreseeability of *physical* injury alone (no need to foresee the psychiatric harm itself), while imposing control devices on secondary victims. Adopting Lord Goff's critique (dissent in *White*), the CA **does not follow** this majority view: the practical difficulty of defining and identifying primary/secondary victims, the arbitrary results turning on how the facts are characterised, and the absence of any justice or logic in favouring primary victims. [para 66–95]
- Accordingly, every victim must prove, as to psychiatric harm, **factual foreseeability + proximity (including the three elements)**. [para 110]

### 5. Shock must come from direct perception — limits of third-party communication [para 132, 135, 138–141]
- **Factual foreseeability** [para 132]: that harm would result from the *mere communication of information* boggles the imagination → not satisfied here.
- **Causal proximity (means of communication)** [para 135–141]: traditionally the shock must come from **directly seeing or hearing the event or its immediate aftermath** (*McLoughlin*; Windeyer J in *Mount Isa Mines*). Shock caused by a third party's *communication of information* is in principle not recoverable. Exceptions: ① false communication with **malign intent** (*Wilkinson v Downton* — but this is an intentional tort), ② where a **professional relationship** exists (*Brown v Mount Barker*, *Furniss v Fitchett*). The present case falls within neither exception. [para 138–142]
- **Public policy (second stage)** [para 143–145]: imposing a duty on mere communication of information would chill the everyday activity of **communicating bad news** → absent malign intent, policy denies a duty. *Tame v NSW* (Gummow & Kirby JJ): absent malice, the *messenger of bad news* bears no liability. [para 139, 144–145]

### 6. Application — the second appellant fails [para 131–142]
- Factual foreseeability is lacking (mere communication) [para 132], and although **relational proximity exists** (the spousal relationship) [para 133], **physical and causal proximity are lacking** (she was not at the scene, and the cause of harm was the *communication* rather than the accident) → **legal proximity not satisfied** → no duty of care. Policy also points toward denying a duty (an unnecessary holding that reinforces the conclusion). [para 134, 142–145]

## Exam points / significance

- **Psychiatric harm ≠ a separate test**: the key point is that the single two-stage [[cases/spandeck-v-dsta|Spandeck]] test applies as is. (Preliminary threshold: recognisable psychiatric illness + factual foreseeability → first-stage proximity → second-stage public policy.)
- **Repositioning McLoughlin's three elements**: *class / proximity to accident / means of shock*, formerly classified as policy factors, are in fact **proximity (first stage)** factors (relational, physical, causal). Only *public policy* remains, located at the second stage.
- **Singapore does not adopt the primary/secondary victim distinction of Page v Smith** — a difference often examined in answers.
- **Psychiatric harm caused by communication is in principle not recoverable** — memorise this with the exceptions (malign intent / professional relationship). *Nervous shock* must come from direct perception (sight/hearing of the event or its immediate aftermath).
- *Pang Koi Fa v Lim Djoe Phing* (applying *McLoughlin*) is approved, but its three elements are reinterpreted as proximity.
