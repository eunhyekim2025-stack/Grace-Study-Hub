# Spandeck Engineering v Defence Science & Technology Agency [2007] SGCA 37

> **Court**: Court of Appeal (Chan Sek Keong CJ, Andrew Phang Boon Leong JA, V K Rajah JA) · **Decision**: 2007-08-08 · Judgment by: Chan Sek Keong CJ
> The leading case establishing a **single universal test** for determining a **duty of care** in Singapore [[law-concepts/ch06-negligence/negligence|negligence]] law. It set out a **two-stage test (proximity → policy) plus a preliminary threshold of factual foreseeability** that applies regardless of the type of damage (pure economic loss vs physical damage).

## One-line Holding

A **single test** applicable to all negligence claims *regardless of the type* of damage is desirable — that is, (i) *factual foreseeability* as a **preliminary threshold**, then (ii) **stage 1, legal proximity**, and (iii) **stage 2, policy considerations**. Here, an elaborate contractual matrix including a contractual arbitration clause existed between the contractor (Spandeck) and the certifier (DSTA), so **proximity was lacking**, and even if proximity were found, **as a matter of policy** no duty of care should be imposed → appeal dismissed. [para 108–116]

## Facts [para 3–20]

- The Government (Employer/Mindef) commissioned the redevelopment of medical facilities at Nee Soon army camp (about S$31.78m), and Spandeck (the contractor) won the bid with an alternative tender. The contract incorporated the **PSSCOC** (Public Sector Standard Conditions of Contract). [para 3–4]
- Under the contract, the **Superintending Officer (SO)** managed and supervised the contractor's work and **certified** interim payments. The SO was initially QKS (a Mindef officer), then in 2000 **DSTA (the respondent)** took over the SO role. [para 4, 6–7]
- Contract cl 2.8: the SO owes no duty to the contractor to exercise its powers. cl 32.8: the Employer is not liable to compensate the contractor for losses caused by delayed or failed certification. cl 34: disputes may be referred to **arbitration**. [para 4–5]
- The contractor argued that the SO/Consultant negligently **under-certified** the work, throwing it into cash-flow difficulties, and that it consequently lost its right to dispute the matter in arbitration when it **novated** the contract to NeoCorp in 2001 → claim against DSTA in **tort (negligence)** for damages (about S$3.8m). [para 12–20]

## Court Below (High Court, [2007] 1 SLR 720) [para 20, 23–24]

- The trial judge: DSTA **owed no duty of care** to the contractor to avoid pure economic loss. The same conclusion was reached applying both the *Anns* two-stage test and the *Caparo* three-part test. Even if a duty existed, there was no evidence of breach. [para 20, 23]
- Grounds: DSTA's lack of a direct assumption of responsibility, the absence of a direct contractual relationship, the contractor's possession of contractual means to claim for variation works, and the fact that the subject was an army camp rather than a residential development. [para 24]

## Issues [para 21–22, 25]

1. What is the **applicable test** for determining the existence of a duty of care — should a *different test* apply to pure economic loss and to physical damage? [para 25, 64]
2. Does a **duty of care arise** between the contractor and the certifier in this case (proximity · policy)? [para 22]

## Holding

Appeal **dismissed**. The court adopted a **single two-stage test (+ preliminary factual foreseeability)** for the existence of a duty of care. Here, substantially as in *Pacific Associates Inc v Baxter* [1990] 1 QB 993, the contractual matrix including an arbitration clause meant **proximity was lacking** → no duty of care. As there was no duty, breach, causation, and remoteness were not decided. [para 108–116]

## Ratio / Core Doctrine

### 1. Adoption of a single, universal test [para 71–73, 115]
- English law was in a **state of flux** through *Anns* (two-stage) → *Murphy* overruling *Anns* → *Caparo* (three-part test: foreseeability + proximity + fair, just and reasonable) → the **exclusionary rule** for pure economic loss, and so on. Prior Singapore authority had also applied a **dual test** — the *Ocean Front* "two-stage process" for pure economic loss and the *Caparo* three-part test for physical damage. [para 31–63]
- The CA **rejected applying different tests by type of damage** — it is not the *type of damage itself* but the *circumstances* in which the loss arose that require different treatment (Bernstein). It therefore applies a **single test** to both pure economic loss and physical damage. [para 64–72]

### 2. **The Spandeck test — the core formulation** [para 73, 115]
> "a coherent and workable test can be fashioned out of the basic **two-stage test premised on proximity and policy considerations**, if its application is **preceded by a preliminary requirement of factual foreseeability** … applied **incrementally**." [para 73]

- **Preliminary threshold — Factual Foreseeability** [para 75–76]: the *factual* question of whether the defendant ought to have known that its carelessness would cause the plaintiff loss. As it is satisfied in almost every case it is not included in the *legal* test, but if it is not satisfied the claim cannot even get off the ground (threshold). It is *foreseeability of the kind of losses*, not a remoteness test. [para 89]
- **Stage 1 — Legal Proximity** [para 77–82]: focuses on the *closeness and directness* of the parties' relationship. The court adopted Deane J's analysis (*Sutherland*) — proximity includes **physical, circumstantial, and causal** proximity, and where the facts support it, the twin criteria of **voluntary assumption of responsibility** + **reliance** are central. [para 81]
- **Stage 2 — Policy Considerations** [para 83–85]: if stage 1 is passed, a *prima facie* duty arises; the court then considers whether policy should **negate** the duty. A relevant example of policy: the existence of a **contractual matrix** that clearly defines the parties' rights and obligations. Public policy must be *distinguished* from proximity, and the court should not hide "unexpressed motives" within proximity but should **expressly articulate** the policy. [para 84–85]

### 3. Incremental Approach [para 73, 82, 115]
- In applying each stage, proximity and policy conclusions are reached **by analogy with decided cases**. The absence of precedent (a novel situation) is not an absolute bar to finding a duty, but incrementalism functions as a safeguard against *indeterminate liability*. Incrementalism is a *method of applying* general principle, not a separate alternative method. [para 41–43, 73]

### 4. Application to the present case [para 87–116]
- **Factual foreseeability**: satisfied. It was foreseeable that the SO's careless certification would directly deprive the contractor of money owed to it. [para 89]
- **Proximity**: lacking. Substantially the same as *Pacific Associates* — an elaborate contractual framework including the **arbitration clause (cl 34)** existed, so the certifier could not be regarded as having voluntarily assumed responsibility to the contractor or the contractor as having relied solely on it. Despite cl 32.8, the contractor could have claimed the under-certified amount in *arbitration*. [para 97–109]
- **Policy**: even if there were proximity, there were strong policy reasons that it would be inappropriate to *superimpose* a tortious duty over the contractual framework. [para 114]

## Exam Points / Significance

- **Memorise the Spandeck test**: (1) preliminary threshold = factual foreseeability → (2) stage 1 = legal proximity (voluntary assumption of responsibility + reliance; physical, circumstantial, causal) → (3) stage 2 = policy. The whole process is **incremental (analogy with precedent)**. A **single test** regardless of the type of damage.
- The **distinction between proximity and policy** is key — fair/just/reasonable (*Caparo*'s third element) is not made a separate stage but absorbed into proximity (between the parties) and policy (society at large).
- The reasoning that **a contractual matrix blocks a tortious duty** (applying *Pacific Associates*) is a staple of answers on construction, certification, and professional liability. Where *adequate contractual remedies* such as arbitration exist, they push against finding a duty on both proximity and policy.
- Later, [[cases/ngiam-kong-seng-v-lim|Ngiam Kong Seng]] (nervous shock) and [[cases/man-mohan-singh-v-zurich|Man Mohan Singh]] **applied and extended** this single test to the contexts of psychiatric harm and damages respectively.
