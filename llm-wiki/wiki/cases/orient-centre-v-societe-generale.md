# Orient Centre Investments Ltd v Societe Generale [2007] SGCA 24

> **Court**: Court of Appeal (Chan Sek Keong CJ, Andrew Phang Boon Leong JA) · **Decision**: 2007-05-09 · Judgment by: Chan Sek Keong CJ
> The Singapore leading case holding that a **non-reliance clause** and **representation and warranty** embedded in bank account-opening and investment documents bar a customer's subsequent claims against the bank for misrepresentation, breach of fiduciary duty, and negligence by way of **evidential estoppel**. It also sets out the principles of **striking out**.

## One-line holding

Once an investor, in the contract documents signed when buying structured products, **represented and warranted** that **"he did not rely on any representation by SG, assessed suitability himself, and was not in a fiduciary relationship with SG"**, his later assertion that those representations were false (a guarantee of capital preservation and a 10% annual return) is **barred by evidential estoppel**, so there is no reasonable cause of action and striking out is justified — and besides, no **actual loss was incurred or particularised** on the structured products, so the claim fails in any event. [para 50–51, 66]

## Facts [para 3–32]

- The appellants are Orient Centre Investments Ltd ("Orient") and Teo Song Kwang ("Teo"), who claims to be its beneficial owner. The respondent is the French bank **Société Générale ("SG")**. The second defendant, Goh, was SG's vice-president and relationship manager and Orient's investment adviser, but left SG on 2000-05-04 (not a party to this appeal).
- Orient opened an **Investment Account** with SG on 1998-05-18. The appellants allege that Goh induced the account opening by representing that "SG is one of the world's top five banks and has a special strategy that would **ensure** Teo **capital preservation + a 10% annual return**". [para 11]
- The court characterised this representation legally as a **warranty** — a warranty that all investments would achieve capital preservation and a 10% annual return. [para 11]
- The documents Teo signed on account opening: the Mandate, the Telephone/Facsimile Indemnity, and the **Declaration** (containing mail-holding and conclusive-evidence clauses). [para 12–13]
- Orient subsequently invested in five structured products (this appeal concerns four — BBELD, Tiger Note 2, Tokyo Deposit, Tokyo Plus Deposit; the claim on the fifth, USD/4Funds, was withdrawn as it post-dated Goh's departure). Each product contract was densely packed with **representation, warranty, and exclusion clauses**. [para 23–32]
- Claiming to have invested US$9.6m and suffered a loss exceeding US$1m, Orient sued SG and Goh on 2004-08-11 for damages for misrepresentation, breach of fiduciary duty, negligence, and so on. [para 4–5]

## Lower court (High Court, [2006] SGHC 164) [para 8]

- Lai Siu Chiu J **struck out** the structured-product claims on two grounds: ① the cause of action the appellants asserted **contradicted Orient's own written representations and warranties**, and ② Goh's **oral representations were inadmissible under Evidence Act s 94 to contradict the written terms**. [para 8]
- The appellants appealed on six grounds.

## Issues [para 9, 40–42]

1. Do the prior oral representations (capital preservation, 10% return) override or supersede the subsequent **express written terms**?
2. The legal nature of the **non-reliance clause** the bank relies on — is it an exclusion clause, or a representation giving rise to **evidential estoppel**? If the latter, must the estoppel be separately pleaded, and is it subject to UCTA's reasonableness scrutiny?
3. Does the fact that the transactions were "authorised" exclude the negligence claim?
4. Does whether the account is discretionary or non-discretionary determine the scope of duty?
5. Was the striking-out application procedurally too late?

## Holding

Appeal **dismissed in full (with costs)**. The lower court's striking out was justified. [para 67]

## Ratio (core doctrine)

### 1. Non-reliance clause = evidential estoppel, not an exclusion clause [para 42–50]
- The appellants argued that the non-reliance clause was either ① a **non-reliance clause** (→ giving rise to evidential estoppel, so it must be pleaded as a defence) or ② an **exclusion clause** (→ so the burden of proving reasonableness under [[statutes/unfair-contract-terms-act|UCTA]] lies on SG). [para 42]
- The court adopted the English Court of Appeal's *E A Grimstead & Son Ltd v McGarrigan* and the three requirements of *Lowe v Lombank*: where a non-reliance statement is (a) clear and unequivocal, (b) intended by the representor to be acted on by the other party, and (c) believed to be true and acted on by that other party, an **evidential estoppel arises preventing the other party from later asserting that it did in fact rely**. [para 44]
- The essence is therefore not an [[law-concepts/ch11-exemption-clauses/exemption-clause|exclusion clause]] but a **bar on a finding of fact (estoppel)** — that is, it removes the *factual premise itself* of a misrepresentation claim ("relied on the representation"). Whether UCTA applies was held unnecessary to the issues on this appeal (left aside). [para 13]

### 2. Pleading — the "estoppel" label is not required [para 45]
- Even though SG did not expressly name "estoppel" in its defence, it suffices that it pleaded the clauses **for their legal effect**. *Boustead Trading v Arab Malaysian Merchant Bank*: pleadings need state **the material facts giving rise to the legal consequence, not the legal consequence (label) itself**. [para 45]
- Moreover, many of the clauses also bear the character of a **representation and warranty**, which SG invoked in its defence for their effect. [para 46]

### 3. Prior oral representations are overridden and superseded by the express written terms [para 50–53]
- Orient itself represented and warranted in each product contract that "**it did not rely on any representation (written or oral) by SG**, knew it was not a capital-preservation product, assessed suitability itself, and **was not in a fiduciary relationship with SG**" (e.g. ELDMA Art 4.11, Tiger Note 2 IDA Art 4.2, FX standard terms cl 12). [para 25–38, 47–49]
- The combined effect of these express terms creates an **insuperable obstacle** to the claim, even if Goh had made such oral representations. There is thus no need even to determine the factual truth of the representations. [para 50]
- Applying the English *Peekay Intermark Ltd v ANZ Banking Group*: even if one signs documents without reading them, by confirming and returning that one "read and understood" them one is taken to have **understood** the nature of the investment, and cannot claim to have been induced by misrepresentation. [para 51–53] → cf. [[cases/raiffeisen-v-archer-daniels-midland]] is an adjacent case on the same *Peekay* logic.

### 4. "Authorisation ≠ absence of negligence" — but irrelevant here [para 54]
- The fact that the transactions were authorised does not logically exclude a negligence claim (on this point the lower court's reasoning was inaccurate). But since the appellants represented and warranted that they would **not rely**, and there was no loss anyway, this point is peripheral. [para 54]

### 5. The discretionary/advisory account distinction does not determine duty [para 55–58]
- Whether the account is discretionary or advisory, the label does not determine the manager's duties and liabilities. Liability for losses turns on **whether there was negligence or breach of fiduciary duty in the advisory process + whether the exclusion clauses apply**. With structured products, the contract terms speak for themselves. [para 56–58]

### 6. Principles of striking out [para 60–64]
- A claim is struck out only where the pleading is **obviously unsustainable** and litigation is plainly impossible. But where, *after full argument* on the pleading and defence, the court is satisfied that there is no reasonable cause of action (or defence), it may strike out even at an advanced stage of proceedings (*Tapematic*, *Gabriel Peter*). Delay was not a problem here. [para 60–64]

### 7. Decisive — there was no loss at all [para 65–66]
- The actual purpose of each structured product was capital preservation and high yield (e.g. BBELD offered a 29.83% annual enhanced yield), unrelated to the alleged "10% per annum" from Goh, and three of the products were "100% Capital Guaranteed". The appellants **failed even to assert or particularise any capital loss.** With no loss, **the claim fails on this ground alone**. [para 65–66]

## Exam points / significance

- The distinction between **non-reliance clause vs exclusion clause** is key — a non-reliance clause does not *exclude* liability but **bars the fact of reliance (evidential estoppel)**, thereby defeating the very elements of [[law-concepts/ch13-misrepresentation/misrepresentation|misrepresentation]] (reliance / inducement). In an answer one must make **this characterisation** before reaching straight for UCTA.
- The *Grimstead*/*Lowe v Lombank* three requirements + the *Peekay* "signing without reading is still deemed understanding" line are the standard defence in banking and investment disputes.
- Pleading: **material facts, not labels** — pleading the clauses for their effect suffices even without naming estoppel (*Boustead Trading*).
- "Authorisation ≠ absence of negligence" is logically correct but powerless against a non-reliance representation and warranty — a point subordinate to the facts.
- Related Singapore authority: the objective construction of settlement and written-term supremacy shares its spirit with [[cases/gay-choon-ing-v-loh]].

> [!uncertain]
> This judgment **deliberately did not decide whether UCTA's reasonableness scrutiny applies** to a non-reliance clause (para 13: "unnecessary to resolve the issues on this appeal"). Whether a non-reliance clause must pass UCTA therefore remains an *open question* rather than part of the ratio — do not state it conclusively in an answer.
