# Man Financial (S) Pte Ltd v Wong Bark Chuan David [2007] SGCA 53

> **Court**: Court of Appeal (Belinda Ang Saw Ean J, Andrew Phang Boon Leong JA, V K Rajah JA) · **Decision**: 2007-11-29 · Judgment by: Phang JA
> The Singapore leading case on **restraint of trade** in the employment context. It restates the *Nordenfelt* framework of a **legitimate proprietary interest** and **two-limb reasonableness (between the parties + the public)**, and confirms that **"maintaining a stable, trained workforce"** can be a legitimate proprietary interest — even in the absence of protectable confidences. Incidentally, it also classifies the restraint clause as a condition, thereby recognising a right to terminate.

## One-line holding

The non-solicitation clause (Clause C.1) ① protects the legitimate proprietary interest of "maintaining a stable, trained workforce" and ② is reasonable both between the parties and in the public interest, so it is **valid**; moreover, since on the parties' intention it was a **condition**, once the defendant (former CEO) breached it the company could terminate, and therefore **is under no obligation to pay the agreed compensation (about US$1m)**. [para 121, 142, 191–192, 195]

## Facts [para 3–8]

- **Wong (defendant/respondent)** had been MD and CEO of the brokerage firm **Man Financial (plaintiff/appellant)** since 1996. Following the 2005 decision to replace the CEO he resigned immediately, on garden leave with a three-month notice period. [para 3–5]
- After negotiation, the **Termination Agreement** was concluded on 2005-06-23. It included a **non-solicitation clause (Clause C.1, 7 months) and a non-competition clause (Clause C.3, 7 months)**. In return the plaintiff agreed to pay **about S$1m (Compensation)** in Man Group plc shares, goodwill, etc. (but no payment if breached, Clause B.2). [para 5–7]
- In September 2005 it was discovered that the defendant had solicited two of the plaintiff's employees (Tricia and Siang Hwee) over to the competitor Refco and had advised Refco → the plaintiff refused to pay the Compensation. The defendant sued to claim the Compensation. [para 8]

## Lower court (High Court, [2007] 2 SLR 22) [para 9–16]

- The court found that the defendant had prima facie breached Clauses C.1 and C.3. However, holding **both C.1 and C.3 void** (unreasonable restraints of trade), it **severed** the void clauses and ordered the plaintiff to pay the Compensation. [para 9–16]
- It reserved judgment on whether maintaining a stable workforce was a legitimate interest, but held that even if such an interest existed, C.1 was too wide (covering all employees and those who had already left) and C.3 lacked proof of confidential information. → The Court of Appeal reversed, holding C.1 reasonable and valid. [para 11–14]

## Issues [para 20]

(a) Does the restraint-of-trade doctrine apply to the Termination Agreement (threshold)? (b) If so, is Clause C.1 reasonable? (c) If reasonable, what are the legal consequences of its breach?

## Holding

Appeal **allowed**. (a) The doctrine applies — the Termination Agreement is not a settlement but a **new supplementary contract**, so it is subject to the restraint-of-trade doctrine. (b) Clause C.1 is **valid because it protects a legitimate interest and is reasonable**. (c) C.1 is a **condition** and the defendant **breached** it, so the plaintiff could terminate → **the defendant cannot claim the Compensation**. (The lower court's conclusion that C.3 is void is upheld, but severance is unnecessary.) [para 195–196]

## Ratio (core doctrine)

### 1. Threshold — does the restraint-of-trade doctrine apply to a settlement contract? [para 21–68] → [[law-concepts/illegality]]

- This Termination Agreement is **not a settlement** that compromises and resolves an existing dispute, but a **new, independent supplementary contract** governing the termination of the employment relationship (the restraint clauses were *fresh* clauses not in the original employment contract). The restraint-of-trade doctrine therefore applies in full. [para 23–25, 41]
- (Obiter) Two public policies conflict: **Public Policy 1** (invalidating restraints of trade) vs **Public Policy 2** (respecting genuine settlements). For Public Policy 2 to override Public Policy 1, two requirements must be **cumulatively** satisfied: ① the settlement compromises an **existing contractual restraint-of-trade dispute** and ② it is **not tainted by any vitiating factor** (adopting *Panayiotou*). Here ① is not met (a new clause, not an existing dispute), so the doctrine applies in any event. [para 60–67]

### 2. The Nordenfelt framework — two-limb reasonableness [para 69–78] → [[law-concepts/restraint-of-trade]]

- Every restraint of trade is **prima facie void** and valid **only if reasonable** (*Nordenfelt v Maxim Nordenfelt* [1894]). [para 69–70]
- Reasonableness has **two separate, cumulative facets**: ① reasonableness as between the **interests of the parties** and ② reasonableness in the **interests of the public**. Following *Thomas Cowan v Orme* (Chua J), Singapore **clearly distinguishes** the two facets (England somewhat conflates them). The burden lies on the party relying on the clause. Reasonableness is assessed **at the time the contract is made**. [para 71–77]
- The employment context is scrutinised **far more strictly** than the sale-of-business context (a purchaser has bought goodwill, whereas employment typically involves an imbalance of bargaining power). [para 48]

### 3. Legitimate Proprietary Interest [para 79–124]

- **A mere bar on competition (a covenant in gross) is impermissible** — there must be a *legitimate interest* to protect, and even where one exists the clause must **not go beyond what is necessary** for that protection. [para 79]
- The two traditional interests in the employment context: **trade secrets** and **trade connection**. [para 81]
  - Trade secrets: identified by the *Faccenda Chicken* four factors (nature of employment, nature of the information, whether confidentiality was impressed, severability). Information in the public domain and mere general skill/knowledge cannot be protected — distinguish a protectable interest from "restricting the employee's own deployment of skill and talent". Express pleading is required (*Stratech*). [para 83–92]
- **The key refinement — the third interest, "maintaining a stable, trained workforce"**: besides trade secrets and trade connection, *other* legitimate interests may exist. Adopting the analysis in the Australian *Cactus Imaging* (Brereton J), the court confirmed that **maintaining workforce stability** is a legitimate interest protectable by a non-solicitation clause — and one that arises **even in the absence of protectable confidences**. Employees are not the company's "property", but like trade connection they form **part of the value of the business (part of its goodwill)** and so are worth protecting. [para 96–122]
- However, recognition of this interest does not make a non-solicitation clause *automatically* reasonable — it must still pass *Nordenfelt* two-limb reasonableness, and the absence of protectable confidences weighs **against** reasonableness. A broad clause covering **"any employee"** regardless of seniority or importance is usually too wide (*TSC Europe* and *Hanover Insurance*). [para 106–110, 121]
- The contrary English line (*Kores* and *Hanover Insurance*: workforce stability is not a legitimate interest) is rejected as **circular reasoning** — assuming "there is no interest" predetermines the conclusion. [para 100–103]

### 4. Application — Clause C.1 is reasonable and valid [para 134–151]

- As former CEO the defendant **held and exploited confidential knowledge** of the relationships with employees in soliciting them, so a legitimate proprietary interest (workforce stability + confidential knowledge) existed. The lower court's "no interest" finding is reversed. [para 136]
- **Reasonableness between the parties**: agreed seriously and without any vitiating factor (the defendant himself proposed shortening it from one year to seven months), substantial consideration (about S$1m) received, and on the wording it targeted **only the solicitation of senior personnel** over whom the defendant had exercised influence, not *peripheral* support staff (contextual construction). The defendant also agreed to and acknowledged the seven-month period. [para 137–142]
- **Reasonableness in the public interest**: consistent with Public Policy 2 (respecting the product of a settlement/negotiation). → C.1 passes both facets and is **valid**. [para 136, 145, 151]
- (Note) Acknowledgment clauses such as C.4 ("this clause is reasonable and necessary"), or "read-down if void" clauses, **add nothing of substance** to the reasonableness inquiry — an unreasonable clause cannot be salvaged by such wording. [para 146–149]

### 5. Consequences of breach — Clause C.1 is a condition [para 152–192] → [[law-concepts/discharge]], [[law-concepts/terms]]

- Citing the four Situations and their ranking from [[cases/rdc-concrete-v-sato-kogyo|RDC]]. C.1 is not Situation 1 or 2 → examine 3(a) (condition) first. [para 153–158, 175–177]
- **Four factors for identifying a condition (added by this judgment to RDC)** [para 159–174]:
  1. **Statute** classifies the clause as a condition (e.g. the Sale of Goods Act) [para 162–164]
  2. The clause **itself states it is a "condition"** (but cf. *Schuler* — it may carry an everyday meaning) [para 165–169]
  3. There is **precedent** [para 171–172]
  4. The transaction is a **mercantile transaction** — a tendency to classify as condition for certainty [para 173]
  - The four factors are non-exhaustive; ultimately the task is to **objectively construe the parties' intention from the contract as a whole**. [para 174]
- Here none of the four factors applies directly, but on the **course and context of negotiation** C.1 is a condition: it is the core of the consideration for the Compensation (C.1–C.3 making up most of it), the defendant fixated on its scope, and **Clause B.2** ("no payment if *any* provision of this agreement is breached") is decisive. → **C.1 = condition**. [para 178–190]
- Accordingly, by the defendant's breach of C.1 the plaintiff could terminate, and the defendant **cannot claim the Compensation** (no need to examine Situation 3(b)). [para 191–192]

## Exam points / significance

- Skeleton for a restraint-of-trade answer: **(0) does the doctrine apply → (1) legitimate proprietary interest → (2) two-limb reasonableness (between the parties + public)**. With no interest, there is no need to reach reasonableness — it is void (covenant in gross).
- The confirmation that **maintaining workforce stability** is — even absent protectable confidences — a **third legitimate proprietary interest** is the key contribution of this judgment (in addition to trade secrets and trade connection).
- An indiscriminate "any employee" non-solicitation clause is too wide; to be reasonable it must be **limited to senior personnel**, accounting for seniority and influence.
- The **four factors** for identifying a condition are cited verbatim by [[cases/sports-connection-v-deuter]] — they are used together in discharge/terms questions.
- Settlement contracts and restraint of trade: whether the Termination Agreement is a settlement or a new contract determines which doctrine applies (contrast the discussion of settlement contracts in [[cases/gay-choon-ing-v-loh]]).
