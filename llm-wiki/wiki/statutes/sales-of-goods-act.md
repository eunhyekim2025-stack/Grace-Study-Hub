# Sale of Goods Act (SOGA, Cap 393)

> **Origin**: adopted from the U.K. 1979 (c. 54), Revised Edition 1999 (in force 1993-11-12; implied terms modernised by Act 43/96 in 1996). A successor to the U.K. 1893 codification (Chalmers). The exam core in contract law is the **implied terms (ss 12-15)** and **conditions vs warranties (ss 10-11, 15A)**.

## Purpose & Scope

- **Long title**: an Act to consolidate the law relating to the sale of goods.
- **Contract of sale (s 2)**: a contract by which the seller **transfers or agrees to transfer the property in goods** to the buyer for a *money consideration, the price*. If transfer is immediate it is a "sale"; if future or conditional it is an "agreement to sell" (s 2(4)-(5)).
- It applies only to *goods* (movable property) — land, services, and choses in action are excluded. Barter and gifts, which are not an exchange of goods for a money price, fall outside SOGA.
- **Preservation of common law (s 62(2))**: common-law rules on agency, fraud, **misrepresentation, duress, and mistake** and other vitiating factors continue to apply to the sale of goods so far as not inconsistent with SOGA → preserving [[law-concepts/ch13-misrepresentation/misrepresentation]], [[law-concepts/ch12-mistake/mistake]], and [[law-concepts/ch14-duress-undue-influence/duress]].

## Key Provisions (Summary of Main Sections)

### Conditions and Warranties

| Section | Heading | Gist |
|---|---|---|
| s 10 | Stipulations as to time | The time of payment is *not, as a rule,* of the essence of the contract; whether other stipulations as to time are of the essence depends on construction of the contract. |
| **s 11** | When a condition is treated as a warranty | (1) The buyer may **elect to treat a breach of condition as a breach of warranty**, or may waive the condition. (2) Whether a stipulation is a **condition** (breach gives a right to terminate) or a **warranty** (damages only, no rejection/termination) is a matter of *construction of the contract* — judged by substance, not by the label. (3) In a **non-severable contract, once the buyer has accepted the goods**, even a breach of condition can be treated only as a breach of warranty (loss of the right to reject). |

### Implied Terms (ss 12-15) — the Core of SOGA

| Section | Heading | Gist |
|---|---|---|
| **s 12** | Title | (1) An **implied *condition*** that the seller has a **right to sell** the goods. (2) Implied warranties that the goods are free from undisclosed encumbrances and that the buyer will enjoy **quiet possession**. → A defect of title is the most fundamental breach. |
| **s 13** | Sale by description | In a sale by description, an implied *condition* that the goods **correspond with the description**. Where sold by sample and description, the goods must correspond with both (s 13(2)). |
| **s 14** | Quality or fitness | As a rule *caveat emptor* — no implied condition as to quality or fitness (s 14(1)). **But where the seller sells in the course of a business**: (s 14(2)) an implied *condition* that the goods are of **satisfactory quality** — the *standard a reasonable person would regard as satisfactory* (taking account of description, price, and other circumstances, s 14(2A); including fitness, appearance, freedom from defects, safety, and durability, s 14(2B)). (s 14(3)) Where the buyer has **made known a particular purpose**, an implied *condition* that the goods are **reasonably fit for that purpose** (except where the buyer did not rely on the seller's skill or judgment). |
| **s 15** | Sale by sample | In a sale by sample, an implied *condition* that (a) the bulk corresponds with the sample in quality, and (c) the goods are free from any defect making their quality unsatisfactory that would not be apparent on reasonable examination. |
| s 15A | Slight breach, non-consumer | Where a **non-consumer** buyer has a right to reject for breach of the ss 13-15 conditions but the breach is so **slight** that rejection would be unreasonable, the breach is treated as a breach of **warranty** rather than a condition (no rejection, damages only). The seller bears the burden of proving slightness (s 15A(3)). |

> **Exclusion of implied terms (s 55)**: rights, duties, and liabilities implied by law **may be excluded or varied by express agreement or course of dealing** (s 55(1)). *However*, this freedom is limited by [[statutes/unfair-contract-terms-act|UCTA]] s 6 — an exclusion of s 12 (title) is wholly void, an exclusion of ss 13-15 against a consumer is also wholly void, and against a non-consumer it is subject to the reasonableness test.

### Other (For Reference)

- **Transfer of property (ss 16-20)**: specific goods pass at the time the parties *intend* (s 17), and where intention is unclear, by Rules 1-5 (s 18). Risk, as a rule, passes with property (s 20).
- **Sale by a non-owner (ss 21-26)**: the *nemo dat quod non habet* principle (s 21) and its exceptions (voidable title s 23, seller/buyer in possession ss 24-25).
- **Performance (ss 27-37)**: delivery and payment are concurrent conditions (s 28); errors in quantity delivered (s 30); acceptance (s 35) — linked to the conversion of a condition into a warranty on acceptance (s 11(3)).
- **Remedies (ss 49-53)**: the seller's action for the price and damages (ss 49-50), the buyer's damages and specific performance (ss 51-52), and the remedy for breach of warranty (s 53). → [[law-concepts/ch18-remedies/remedies]], [[law-concepts/ch16-breach/discharge]].

## Role in Contract Law

- **Statutory source of implied terms**: it lays down terms that are automatically incorporated into a sale contract even where the parties do not express them — the leading example of *terms implied by statute* under the wiki's [[law-concepts/ch10-terms/terms]] (express vs implied terms).
- **Pivot of the condition/warranty dichotomy**: since SOGA characterises ss 12-15 all as **"conditions"**, a breach gives the buyer the right to *reject the goods and repudiate the contract* (s 11). This connects directly with [[law-concepts/ch16-breach/discharge]] (termination of performance for breach) — a breach of condition is the strongest category of termination ground under the *RDC Concrete* breach classification. cf. [[cases/rdc-concrete-v-sato-kogyo]].
- **The mandatory core of consumer protection**: s 55 (freedom to exclude) is limited by UCTA, so the key implied terms in title and consumer transactions operate as *non-excludable mandatory rules* → a pair with [[statutes/unfair-contract-terms-act]].
- **Capacity**: s 3 delegates capacity to sell to the general law of contract but provides that a minor or mentally incapacitated person who has been supplied with *necessaries* must pay a reasonable price → [[law-concepts/ch09-capacity-privity/capacity]].
