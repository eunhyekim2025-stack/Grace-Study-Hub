# Contributory Negligence and Personal Injuries Act

> Cap. 54 (2002 Rev Ed). Originally enacted as Ordinance 37 of 1953, in force 1954-03-01. The Singapore version of the UK Law Reform (Contributory Negligence) Act 1945. A short statute of 5 sections.

## Purpose and Scope

Governs the **apportionment of damages** where the claimant's own fault has **partly contributed** to the harm. At common law, contributory negligence was a *complete defence*, so a claimant with even the slightest fault lost their entire claim. This Act abolishes that "all-or-nothing" rule and replaces it with **reduction in proportion to responsibility** (governs the defence/remedy aspects of [[law-concepts/ch06-negligence/negligence]], linked to [[law-concepts/ch18-remedies/remedies]]).

- **Definition of "fault"** (s.2): negligence, breach of statutory duty, or any act or omission that *gives rise to tortious liability or would, apart from this Act, give rise to the defence of contributory negligence*. → Pure breach of contract is not, in principle, included.
- **"damage"** (s.2): includes death and personal injury.
- **Exclusion** (s.4): does not apply to claims to which s.1 of the Maritime Conventions Act 1911 applies (collisions at sea, etc.).

## Key Provisions (Summary of Main Sections)

| Section | Content |
|----|------|
| **s.3(1)** | **Core provision.** Where harm results *partly* from the claimant's own fault and *partly* from another's fault, the claim is **not defeated** by reason only of the claimant's contributory negligence. However, damages are **reduced** to such extent as the court thinks "just and equitable" having regard to the **claimant's share in responsibility**. |
| **s.3(2)** | This section does not defeat any defence arising under a contract (contractual defences preserved). |
| **s.3(3)** | Where a contract or statute limiting liability applies, damages after reduction cannot exceed that **cap**. |
| **s.3(4)** | The court must **record the total damages (full amount)** that would have been recoverable *but for* the contributory negligence (stating the pre-reduction figure). |
| **s.3(5)** | In a death case, where the deceased was contributorily negligent, damages in dependants'/relatives' claims (Civil Law Act s.20, s.21) are also **reduced proportionately**. |
| **s.5** | **Abolition of the common employment defence.** An employer cannot rely on the "common employment" defence against an employee injured by a fellow employee's negligence (5(1)), and any term of an employment contract excluding/limiting this liability is void (5(2)). |

## Role in Contract Law / Tort Law

The centre of gravity of this Act is in **tort — especially [[law-concepts/ch06-negligence/negligence]]**. The key operation in a Business Law context is:

1. **Establish the defendant's liability → assess the claimant's contributory negligence → reduce proportionately** (it intervenes directly at the damages-assessment stage of [[law-concepts/ch18-remedies/remedies]]).
2. Contributory negligence is now a *ground for reduction*, not a *defence*. So in exam answers, you conclude not with "claim dismissed because the claimant was partly at fault" but with "claimant X% responsible → damages reduced by X%."
3. Because of **s.3(2)**, *contractual* defences (e.g., a valid [[law-concepts/ch11-exemption-clauses/exemption-clause]]) survive — i.e., this Act does not break contractual defences.

> [!uncertain] Whether the contributory negligence doctrine applies to *breach of contract* claims is contested at common law (the three-category analysis in the UK case Vesta v Butcher). Like the FCA, this Act's definition of "fault" is tort-centred, and extension to pure contract claims should be treated with caution.
