# Consumer Protection (Fair Trading) Act (CPFTA)

> Cap. 52A (2009 Rev Ed, 4/8/2016 operative edition). Originally enacted as Act 27 of 2003, in force 2004-03-01. Major amendments: Act 15/2008 (strengthened procedure and enforcement), **Act 7/2012 (introduced Part III "Lemon Law", in force 2012-09-01)**. Purpose: to protect consumers from unfair practices and to grant additional rights in respect of goods that do not conform to the contract.

## Purpose and Scope

Two pillars of consumer protection: **(1) regulation of unfair practices** (Part II), and **(2) additional rights for non-conforming goods = Lemon Law** (Part III). False and misleading dealings are a *statutory extension* of [[law-concepts/misrepresentation]], while one-sided/oppressive terms and undue pressure are the consumer-law counterparts of [[law-concepts/unconscionability]] and [[law-concepts/undue-influence]].

- **"consumer"** (s.2): an **individual** who receives (or has the right to receive, or is liable to pay for) goods or services *otherwise than exclusively in the course of business*. → Pure B2B transactions are excluded.
- **"supplier" / "goods" / "services"** (s.2): a supplier is a person who supplies, manufactures, or promotes goods/services in the course of business. "goods" includes residential property, vouchers, and even financial products.
- **Territorial application** (s.3): the supplier or consumer must be resident in Singapore, or the offer/acceptance must be made in or from Singapore.
- **Exclusions** (First Schedule, s.2): acquisition of an interest in land (except residential leases and time shares), services under a contract of employment, etc.

## Key Provisions (Summary of Main Sections)

### Part II — Unfair Practices

| Section | Content |
|----|------|
| **s.4** | **Definition of "unfair practice."** (a) an act or omission that a consumer might reasonably be *deceived or misled by*; (b) a false claim; (c) knowingly **taking advantage** of a consumer who is not in a position to protect their own interests or does not understand the transaction; (d) conduct listed in the Second Schedule. |
| **Second Schedule** | **20 specific unfair practices** (s.4(d)). E.g.: representing non-existent sponsorship/performance (§1), representing goods as new when they are not (§3), **inserting harsh, oppressive, excessively one-sided, or unconscionable terms (§11)**, **exerting undue pressure or undue influence (§12)**, concealing a material fact in fine print (§20). |
| **s.6** | **Consumer's right to sue.** A consumer in a transaction involving an unfair practice may sue the supplier. Cap of **$30,000** (s.6(6)). No relief for goods/services for business use (s.7(6)). |
| **s.7** | **Jurisdiction and court powers.** The Small Claims Tribunal also has jurisdiction (s.7(1)). The court (other than the STC) may order (a) restitution, (b) damages, (c) specific performance, (d) repair/parts, (e) variation of the contract (s.7(4)). The claim is *deemed to be founded on contract* (s.7(3)). |
| **s.8** | Voluntary compliance agreement — a specified body obtains a supplier's undertaking to cease an unfair practice. |
| **s.9–10** | Declarations and injunctions by the District/High Court. The Injunction Proposals Review Panel reviews and approves applications. |
| **s.11** | Power to make regulations granting a **right to cancel within a cancellation period** for certain classes of contracts. |
| **s.12** | **Limitation period.** A s.6 action must be brought within **2 years** of the last material event or of the date the consumer became aware of the unfair practice. |

### Part III — Additional Rights for Non-Conforming Goods (Lemon Law, ss.12A–12F)

Introduced in 2012. Provides tiered remedies where, **at the time of delivery, goods do not conform (non-conforming) to the contract** under a sale, transfer of goods, or hire-purchase (applicable contract, s.12A).

| Section | Content |
|----|------|
| **s.12A(4)** | Non-conformity = breach of Sale of Goods Act s.13/14/15 (or the corresponding implied terms under the Supply of Goods Act / Hire-Purchase Act) or of an express term. |
| **s.12B(3)** | **Six-month presumption rule.** If non-conformity appears within 6 months of delivery, *it is presumed to have existed at the time of delivery* (reversal of the burden of proof — the supplier must rebut). |
| **s.12C** | **First-tier remedy: repair or replace.** Within a reasonable time, without significant inconvenience, with costs (labour, materials, postage) borne by the supplier. Cannot be claimed if impossible or disproportionate. |
| **s.12D** | **Second-tier remedy: reduction or rescission.** Available where repair/replacement is impossible or disproportionate, or the supplier fails to perform within a reasonable time. On rescission, the refund is reduced by the benefit the consumer has used (s.12D(3)). |
| **s.12E** | Provides the **order (hierarchy) of remedies** — repair/replacement must first be given a reasonable time before moving to the next tier. |
| **s.12F** | Court powers — may order specific performance, or proceed with an appropriate remedy *different* from the one the consumer requested. |

### Part IV — General

- **s.13 No contracting out**: any contract term or waiver of rights contrary to this Act is void (except a release in settlement of a dispute). → Mandatory provision.
- **s.16**: a publisher who prints/publishes an advertisement in good faith is exempt from liability.
- **s.17 Abolition of the parol evidence rule**: in consumer transactions, the existence of an express warranty may be proved by oral/extrinsic evidence *even against a written contract* (displacing Evidence Act ss.93–94).
- **s.18**: where a contract document is ambiguous, it is **construed against the supplier** (statutory contra proferentem).
- **s.18A Burden of proof**: where compliance with this Act is disputed, *the burden of proving compliance lies on the supplier*.

## Role in Contract Law

The CPFTA **extends and strengthens** common law [[law-concepts/misrepresentation]] in the consumer sphere:

1. **Captures omissions and "taking advantage"** (s.4(a),(c)) — common law misrepresentation requires a positive false statement, but the CPFTA treats even *omissions* that mislead the consumer and the *exploitation* of vulnerability as unfair practices.
2. **Diversifies remedies** (s.7(4)) — in addition to damages, restitution, specific performance, and variation. This broadens the menu of [[law-concepts/remedies]].
3. **Limits freedom of contract** (s.13 mandatory, s.17 abolition of parol evidence, s.18 contra proferentem) — mandatory protection skewed against the supplier.
4. **Lemon Law** (Part III) — operationalises the implied conditions of the Sale of Goods Act into a clear remedial ladder of *repair → replace → reduction/rescission* and reverses the burden of proof for six months.

Second Schedule §11 (unconscionable terms) and §12 (undue pressure) **codify** [[law-concepts/unconscionability]] and [[law-concepts/undue-influence]] **as statutory unfair practices** — i.e., common law vitiating factors become subject to administrative and civil enforcement in the consumer context.
