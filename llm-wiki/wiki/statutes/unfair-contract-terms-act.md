# Unfair Contract Terms Act (UCTA, Cap 396)

> **Origin**: the U.K. 1977 (c. 50), received into Singapore via the Application of English Law Act (Cap 7A), Revised Edition 1994 (in force 1993-11-12). Part II (ss 15-25, relating to Scotland) is **excluded**. In practice, Part I (ss 1-14) is the core.

## Purpose & Scope

- **Long title**: an Act to impose *further limits* on the extent to which civil liability for breach of contract, negligence, or other breach of duty **can be avoided by contract terms or notices**.
- UCTA either **renders exemption clauses void or subjects them to the reasonableness test**. It thus operates in two stages: (1) some exemptions are **wholly void**, (2) the rest are **valid only if reasonable**.
- **Confined to "business liability" (s 1(3))**: ss 2-7 apply, as a rule, only to liability for breach of duty arising **in the course of a business**. They do not apply to purely private transactions between individuals.
- **Exclusions (First Schedule)**: ss 2-4 do not apply to contracts of insurance, the creation or transfer of interests in land, intellectual property, the formation/dissolution/constitution of companies, securities, etc. **International supply contracts (s 26)** and **avoidance by choice of governing law (s 27)** are treated separately.

## Key Provisions (Summary of Main Sections)

| Section | Heading | Gist |
|---|---|---|
| **s 2** | Negligence liability | (1) Liability for **death or personal injury caused by negligence** **cannot be excluded or restricted at all** by a contract term or notice. (2) Negligence liability for other loss may be excluded or restricted **only so far as it satisfies the reasonableness requirement**. (3) *Agreement to or awareness of* an exemption clause is not in itself a voluntary assumption of risk (volenti). |
| **s 3** | Liability arising in contract | **Where one party deals as a consumer or on the other's *written standard terms*** — the other party cannot (a) exclude or restrict liability for its own breach, or (b) claim to be entitled (i) to render performance *substantially different* from what was reasonably expected, or (ii) to render no performance at all — **except so far as the term satisfies the reasonableness requirement.** |
| **s 4** | Unreasonable indemnity clauses | A person dealing as a consumer cannot be made to **indemnify** another against that other's liability for negligence or breach of contract, except so far as the term satisfies the reasonableness requirement. |
| s 5 | Guarantee of consumer goods | Liability for loss arising from defects or negligence while goods are in consumer use cannot be excluded or restricted by a term in a **guarantee**. |
| **s 6** | Sale and hire-purchase | (1) Liability for breach of the **SOGA s 12 (title)** implied condition **cannot be excluded against anyone at all.** (2) Against a consumer, liability for breach of **SOGA ss 13-15 (description, quality, fitness, sample)** **also cannot be excluded at all.** (3) Against a non-consumer, ss 13-15 liability can be excluded **only so far as it satisfies the reasonableness requirement**. → directly linked to [[statutes/sales-of-goods-act]]. |
| s 7 | Other contracts for the transfer of goods | Controls parallel to s 6 for contracts transferring goods to which the sale/hire-purchase law does not apply (e.g., leases, ancillary services). |
| s 9 | Effect of breach | A term that requires reasonableness may still be effective even though the contract has been terminated by breach or rescission. |
| s 10 | Evasion by means of a secondary contract | A person cannot circumvent the controls of this Act by means of a secondary contract that takes away rights under another contract. |
| **s 11** | **Reasonableness test** | (1) For a term to be reasonable, it must have been **fair and reasonable to include** in light of the circumstances known or contemplated by the parties at the time of contracting. (2) For ss 6 and 7, the **Second Schedule guidelines** apply. (4) Where liability is limited to a *sum of money*, regard is had to available resources and the availability of insurance. (5) **The burden of proving reasonableness is on the party claiming it (= the party relying on the exemption).** |
| **s 12** | Definition of dealing as a consumer | "deals as consumer": (a) the party does not make the contract in the course of a business nor hold itself out as doing so, (b) the other party makes the contract in the course of a business, and (c) (in the case of sale/hire-purchase of goods) the goods are of a type ordinarily for private use or consumption. **A buyer at auction or competitive tender is not a consumer (s 12(2)).** The burden of proving that a party is not a consumer is on the party so claiming (s 12(3)). |
| s 13 | Varieties of exemption clause | Limiting liability, subjecting it to onerous conditions, excluding rights or remedies, or excluding rules of evidence/procedure are all treated as exemptions (anti-circumvention). |
| s 14 | Definitions | "business" includes a profession and a government department; "negligence" has the meaning in s 1(1). |

> **Second Schedule (reasonableness-test guidelines, for ss 6 and 7)**: (a) the relative **strength of the parties' bargaining positions**, (b) whether the customer received an *inducement* or had an opportunity to contract elsewhere, (c) whether the customer **knew or ought to have known** of the term's existence and extent (considering trade practice and prior dealings), (d) whether it was reasonably practicable to comply with a condition whose non-compliance would exclude liability, (e) whether the goods were made to the customer's *special order*.

## Role in Contract Law

- **Statutory framework for controlling exemption clauses**: the common law controls exemption clauses through **incorporation** and **construction (contra proferentem)** but cannot control the fairness of the *content itself*. UCTA fills that gap — some are wholly void, the rest subject to the reasonableness test — introducing **substantive control**. → the final gate in the three-stage analysis of [[law-concepts/exemption-clause]] (incorporation → construction → UCTA).
- **The asymmetry of consumer protection**: s 12's "consumer/non-consumer" distinction sets the intensity of control — against a consumer, an exclusion of the SOGA implied terms is *wholly void* (s 6(2)), while against a non-consumer (B2B) it is subject to the *reasonableness test* (s 6(3)). This asymmetry is a frequent issue in exam answers.
- **Separate treatment of negligence (s 2)**: an exclusion of liability for death or personal injury is **wholly void** regardless of reasonableness — the limit on avoiding [[law-concepts/negligence]] liability. Only exclusions of liability for property or economic loss are subject to the reasonableness test.
- **Reversal of the burden of proof (ss 11(5), 12(3))**: the party relying on the exemption clause must prove its *reasonableness* — unlike common-law implied-term analysis, the burden lies on the party asserting the exemption.
