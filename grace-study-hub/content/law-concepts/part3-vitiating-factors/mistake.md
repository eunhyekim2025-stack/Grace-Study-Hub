# Mistake

An operative mistake renders a contract **void ab initio**.

## Types

### Common Mistake
Both parties share the **same** false assumption.
- **Non-existence of subject matter (res extincta)**: already perished → *Couturier v Hastie [1856]*
- **Mistake as to ownership (res sua)**: already owned by oneself
- **Mistake as to quality**: as a rule not void; only where fundamentally different → *Bell v Lever Bros [1932]*, *Associated Japanese Bank v Credit du Nord [1989]*

### Mutual Mistake
The parties have **different** things in mind → no meeting of minds. Judged on an objective standard → *Raffles v Wichelhaus [1864]* (the Peerless case)

### Unilateral Mistake
**Only one party** is mistaken, and the other **knows or ought to have known** of the mistake.
- Mistake as to a term → *Hartog v Colin & Shields [1939]*
- Mistake as to identity: in a face-to-face setting, presumed intent to deal with the person present → *Phillips v Brooks [1919]*, *Cundy v Lindsay [1878]*, *Shogun Finance v Hudson [2004]*
- **Singapore equitable mistake**: even without actual knowledge, where there is constructive knowledge + unconscionable conduct, equity may set the contract aside → *Chwee Kin Keong v Digilandmall [2005]* (the mispriced goods case)

### Non Est Factum ("it is not my deed")
A signed document is fundamentally different + no negligence on the signer's part → void → *Saunders v Anglia Building Society [1971]*

---

## Effects
- Common law mistake: **void** (third party rights not protected)
- Equity mistake: **voidable / rescission** (third party rights protected)
