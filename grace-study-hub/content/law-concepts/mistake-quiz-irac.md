# Chapter 8 · Mistake — IRAC Drill

> Each question is decided by **one key fact**. The drill is to find that fact and connect it to the Rule.
> Format: Issue → Rule (case) → Application (fact → law) → Conclusion → exam tip

---

## 1. Common Mistake (Quality) — This Time It's Void

Tony agreed to buy "top-grade carbon-steel alloy" worth $2M from the Nibelung steelworks. After the contract, both parties belatedly discovered that the material delivered was ordinary steel. Unlike the top-grade alloy, ordinary steel **cannot be used** in the aircraft fuselages Tony manufactures, and using it would make it impossible to obtain aviation safety certification.

**Q: Is Common Mistake made out?**

<details>
<summary>▶ Model Answer</summary>

**Issue**: Whether Common Mistake (Broader Doctrine — Quality) is made out

**Rule** (*Bell v Lever Brothers [1932]*, *Associated Japanese Bank v Credit du Nord [1989]*): A mistake as to quality renders a contract void only if it makes the subject matter "essentially and radically different." *Associated Japanese Bank*: the guaranteed machines did not exist, so the very purpose of the guarantee was impossible → void.

**Application**:
- Ordinary steel ≠ top-grade alloy → a difference in quality exists
- For Tony's manufacture of aircraft fuselages, ordinary steel **cannot obtain aviation safety certification** → the very purpose of the contract cannot be achieved
- The "radically different subject matter" test is **satisfied** ← the decisive difference from Scenario A4

**Conclusion**: Common Mistake **made out** → contract **void**. (cf. A4: both usable → not made out)

> **Exam tip**: The Bell test turns on whether the purpose can still be achieved. "Can it be used or not" is the crux. Memorise A4 (usable, not made out) and this problem (unusable, made out) side by side.

</details>

## 2. Common Mistake (Res Extincta) — The McRae Exception

Donald contracted to sell "all the wine stock in Warehouse 7" to Dionysus for $80,000. Just before concluding the contract, Donald told Dionysus, "I checked it myself. There is definitely wine in Warehouse 7." But Warehouse 7 was in fact completely empty. Both parties were unaware of this at the time of contracting.

**Q: Is Res Extincta made out? Who bears the liability?**

<details>
<summary>▶ Model Answer</summary>

**Issue**: Common Mistake (Res Extincta) — but whether the risk-allocation exception applies

**Rule** (*Couturier v Hastie [1856]*, *McRae v Commonwealth Disposals Commission [1951]*): Under Res Extincta, a contract is void where the subject matter does not exist. However, if one party **warrants** the existence of the subject matter, that party has assumed the risk → Res Extincta does not apply, and that party is liable for breach.

**Application**:
- The wine in Warehouse 7 did not exist at the time of contracting → Res Extincta applies on its face
- **However**: just before contracting, Donald stated "I checked it myself; it is definitely there" → he **warranted** the existence of the wine
- Applying *McRae*: the warranting party (Donald) assumed the risk of non-existence → Res Extincta excluded

**Conclusion**: Res Extincta **not made out**. Contract **valid**. Donald is liable for breach of contract → Dionysus can claim damages.

> **Exam tip**: When you see phrases like "it is definitely there" or "I guarantee it," check the McRae exception first. Warranting existence = assuming risk = blocking Res Extincta.

</details>

## 3. Mutual Mistake — Looks Like a Mistake, But Isn't

Sif agreed to sell "Mjolnir Unit 1" to Volstagg for $200,000. The contract simply states "Mjolnir Unit 1." Volstagg later claimed that he had in mind a model manufactured in 2019 while Sif had in mind a model manufactured in 2021. However, only **one** Mjolnir Unit 1 — the 2021 model — exists in Sif's warehouse, and Volstagg had personally seen it during negotiations.

**Q: Is Mutual Mistake made out?**

<details>
<summary>▶ Model Answer</summary>

**Issue**: Mutual Mistake — whether there is a meeting of the minds (agreement)

**Rule** (*Raffles v Wichelhaus [1864]*): Mutual Mistake arises where offer and acceptance point to fundamentally different subject matter. However, if the content of the contract can be ascertained on an objective standard (a reasonable third party), there is no mistake → the contract is valid.

**Application**:
- Volstagg's subjective intention (2019 model) ≠ Sif's intention (2021 model)
- **However**: only one model (the 2021 model) exists in the warehouse + Volstagg personally inspected it during negotiations
- On an objective standard, "Mjolnir Unit 1" = the 2021 model can be **ascertained**
- Mutual Mistake arises only where objective interpretation is impossible (*Raffles*: two ships, no external evidence)

**Conclusion**: Mutual Mistake **not made out**. Contract **valid**. Volstagg is obliged to take delivery of the 2021 model.

> **Exam tip**: Even where the parties' subjective intentions differ, Mutual Mistake is blocked if the subject matter can be identified through objective evidence (on-site inspection, documents, industry practice). *Raffles* succeeded only because there was no external evidence at all to distinguish the two "Peerless" ships.

</details>

## 4. Unilateral Mistake (Term) — Is Silence Acceptable or Not?

While selling coffee beans to Borson, Hela offered a delivery unit price of "$50 per kg." The actual market price is $5 per kg. Borson **knew** the price was absurdly high but said nothing and signed the contract. Hela later discovered the mistake and claims the contract is void.

**Q: Is Hela's claim of Unilateral Mistake made out?**

<details>
<summary>▶ Model Answer</summary>

**Issue**: Unilateral Mistake (term) — whether the non-mistaken party (Borson) had actual knowledge

**Rule** (*Smith v Hughes [1871]*, *Chwee Kin Keong v Digilandmall [2005]*): Unilateral Mistake (CL): ① a mistake as to a material term + ② the non-mistaken party has **actual knowledge** → contract void. A case where the non-mistaken party simply exploits the other's error may also amount to "actual knowledge."

**Application**:
- Mistake: "$50 per kg" (10× the actual market price of $5) → a mistake as to the **material term** of price ✓
- Borson **knew** that a price 10× the market rate was an error, yet stayed silent → **actual knowledge** found
- Silence + signature = exploiting the mistake → *Caveat emptor* protects self-induced misjudgement, but does **not** apply where one **knowingly exploits the other's mistake**

**Conclusion**: Unilateral Mistake (CL) **made out** → contract **void**.

> **Exam tip**: Caveat emptor (buyer beware) is the principle that there is no duty to correct "my own misjudgement." But it is different where the other party **knew** of my mistake and stayed silent — that is actual knowledge + exploitation.

</details>

## 5. Unilateral Mistake (Term, Equity) — Could Have Known, But Didn't

Stan sells limited-edition comics online. Due to a staff error, a $3,000 series was displayed as $30. Jack, a long-time expert in the comics industry, knew the usual value of the series well but thought "maybe it's a sale" and ordered 10 sets. There is no evidence that Jack actually "knew" about the error.

**Q: Can Stan rescind the contract? Under which doctrine?**

<details>
<summary>▶ Model Answer</summary>

**Issue**: Unilateral Mistake — no actual knowledge; examine constructive knowledge + impropriety (Equity)

**Rule** (*Chwee Kin Keong v Digilandmall [2005]*): CL requires actual knowledge. Equity: ① a mistake as to a material term + ② **constructive knowledge** — a reasonable person would have known of the mistake + ③ **impropriety (unconscionable conduct)** → contract **voidable**.

**Application**:
- Actual knowledge: no evidence Jack actually knew → CL not made out
- Constructive knowledge: Jack is an industry expert, $30 = 1% of the usual price → **a reasonable expert should have known** it was an error ✓
- Impropriety: ordering 10 sets in bulk while thinking "maybe it's a sale" → conduct seeking to profit while aware of the possibility of an error → **unconscionable conduct can be found** ✓

**Conclusion**: CL not made out. **Equity Unilateral Mistake may be made out** → contract **voidable** (Stan may rescind).

> **Exam tip**: Where an industry expert, an extreme price discrepancy, and a bulk order overlap, consider the Equity claim through the combination of constructive knowledge + impropriety. Note that Equity is voidable, so rescission must occur before a third party acquires in good faith.

</details>

## 6. Mistaken Identity (Face-to-Face) — Exceptionally Void

Janet wanted to sell a rare piece of jewellery. The buyer, Enchantress, came in person, presented an ID, and said, "I am Dr. Strange, a curator at a famous museum. This is an official museum purchase." Janet expressly said, "I won't sell to anyone but Dr. Strange," and contracted after confirming that Enchantress's ID was in Dr. Strange's name. The ID was forged. Enchantress sold the jewellery to Thor.

**Q: Can Janet recover the jewellery from Thor?**

<details>
<summary>▶ Model Answer</summary>

**Issue**: Unilateral Mistake (Mistaken Identity) — face-to-face; whether the presumption is rebutted

**Rule** (*Phillips v Brooks [1919]*, *Ingram v Little [1961]*): In a face-to-face contract the contract is, as a rule, **voidable** (presumption of intent to deal with the person present). However, where the identity of a specific person is expressly stated as a **condition precedent** of the contract, the presumption is rebutted → void (*Ingram v Little*).

**Application**:
- Face-to-face dealing → the Phillips v Brooks presumption applies
- **However**: Janet expressly declared "I won't sell to anyone but Dr. Strange" → identity is a condition precedent of the contract
- She even demanded verification of the ID → it is clear that identity was the **key inducement** of the contract
- Applying *Ingram v Little*: the presumption is rebutted → contract **void**

**Conclusion**: Contract **void** → title never passed to Enchantress → Thor cannot acquire it either → **Janet can recover it**.

> **Exam tip**: Even face-to-face, where there is an express condition of "I won't sell unless it's you," the Ingram v Little exception applies. The difference between Phillips v Brooks (presumption) and Ingram (presumption rebutted) is whether identity was a **condition precedent** of the contract.

</details>

## 7. Non Est Factum — A Case Where It Succeeds

Borson (90 years old, with severe cognitive decline) was told the document was a "consent form to donate to a senior welfare fund" and signed it. In fact, the document was a **deed of gift** transferring his entire property to Hela. Borson could read but his cognitive ability to understand the content was markedly impaired, and Hela deliberately flipped quickly through a document filled with difficult legal terms to induce the signature.

**Q: Can Borson plead Non Est Factum?**

<details>
<summary>▶ Model Answer</summary>

**Issue**: Non Est Factum — whether both requirements are satisfied

**Rule** (*Saunders v Anglia Building Society [1971]*): Requirements for NEF: ① the signed document is **radically different** from what was intended + ② the signer is **not negligent**. General principle: signature = binding (*L'Estrange v Graucob [1934]*).

**Application**:
- ① **Radical difference**: "donation consent form" vs "deed transferring entire property" → radically different in legal nature, effect, and subject matter ✓
- ② **No negligence**:
  - Borson's cognitive decline → unable to understand the content (not a mere eyesight problem)
  - Hela deliberately used complex legal terms + induced a quick signature → a fraudulent method
  - Distinguish from *Saunders*: there the negligence was the signer's **own carelessness** in not wearing glasses. Here there is the other party's active deception + Borson's cognitive decline → fault cannot be attributed to him

**Conclusion**: NEF **made out** → contract **void**.

> **Exam tip**: The comparison with *Saunders* (which failed) is key. Not wearing glasses (own fault) vs cognitive decline + the other party's deception (fault not attributable). When pleading NEF, always argue the negligence point with concrete facts.

</details>

## 8. Common Mistake (Equity) — Voidable, Not Void

Sif and Fandral agreed to trade a rare wine collection for $500,000. After concluding the contract, both discovered that the wine's certificate of origin was forged. The wine itself exists and its quality is not bad, but without the certificate its market value is only $50,000. Fandral had no involvement in forging the certificate.

**Q: What remedy can Sif claim? Discuss separately under Common law and Equity.**

<details>
<summary>▶ Model Answer</summary>

**Issue**: Common Mistake (Quality) — whether void at CL and the possibility of an Equity remedy

**Rule** (*Bell v Lever Brothers [1932]*, *Chwee Kin Keong v Digilandmall [2005]*):
- CL: the mistake must make the subject matter "radically different" to be void — a very narrow requirement
- Equity: if the mistake is "sufficiently serious," it is voidable — more flexible

**Application (CL)**:
- The wine **exists** and retains its quality → the subject matter is not non-existent
- $500,000 → $50,000: a 90% drop in value, but the wine itself exists and is usable
- CL Bell test: hard to see it as "radically different subject matter" → CL void **not made out**

**Application (Equity)**:
- Wine without a certificate can be treated as an **essentially different product** from what the parties contracted for
- 90% drop in value: amounts to a "sufficiently serious mistake"
- Fandral is not at fault → no bar to granting an equitable remedy
- → **voidable in Equity, rescission** available

**Conclusion**: CL not made out. **Voidable for mistake in Equity** → Sif can rescind and claim a refund of the price. However, if Fandral transferred the wine to a third party before rescission, the equitable remedy is barred.

> **Exam tip**: Even if CL fails, always examine Equity. Equity (voidable, flexible remedy) is often easier to make out in practice than CL (void, no third-party protection). Always argue the two branches in parallel.

</details>
