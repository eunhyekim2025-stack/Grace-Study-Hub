# Unconscionability

Equity sets aside a transaction entered into by unconscionably exploiting a weaker party's vulnerability → **voidable**.

## Singapore — Narrow Doctrine (*BOM v BOK [2018] SGCA 83*)

The Singapore Court of Appeal adopted a **narrow** doctrine of unconscionability — refining the *Fry v Lane* / *Cresswell* archetype. The test [[cases/bom-v-bok]] [para 141–142]:

1. **Infirmity of the weaker party**: includes not only poverty and ignorance but also **physical, mental and emotional** vulnerability. However, it must be serious enough to **substantially impair the ability to protect one's own interests**, and must be **evident or ought to have been evident** to the other party.
2. The stronger party **exploited this in procuring the transaction**.
3. → Once established, the burden shifts to the stronger party to prove the transaction was **fair, just and reasonable**.
4. **A substantial undervalue and the absence of independent advice are not essential requirements** — though they are *"very important factors"* the court always considers. [para 141–142]

> *BOM v BOK*: rejected the broad "special disability" doctrine of Australia's *Amadio* as **too subjective**; recognised only the narrow doctrine. [para 132–133]
> Also raised the hypothesis (left undecided) that the narrow doctrine may be **virtually identical to Class 1 undue influence**. [para 145–152] → [[wiki/law-concepts/undue-influence]]

## Traditional test (England)
- The weaker party is "poor and ignorant" → *Cresswell v Potter [1978]*, *Fry v Lane [1888]*
- The transaction is at a substantial undervalue + absence of independent advice

---

## Distinguishing from Duress / Undue Influence
- **Duress**: illegitimate pressure (a threat)
- **Undue influence**: abuse of influence within a relationship
- **Unconscionability**: the unconscionability of the transaction itself + exploitation of the weaker party's vulnerability
